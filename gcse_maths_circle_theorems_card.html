The Seven Circle Theorems

OCR GCSE Maths — know them, spot them, use them

1. Angle at the Centre

CENTRE & CIRCUMFERENCE
The rule
The angle at the centre of a circle is twice the angle at the circumference, when both are subtended by the same arc.
Exam phrase
"The angle at the centre is twice the angle at the circumference."

2. Angle in a Semicircle

DIAMETER = 90°
The rule
The angle at the circumference in a semicircle is always 90°. The triangle must be drawn from both ends of a diameter.
Exam phrase
"The angle in a semicircle is 90°."

3. Angles in the Same Segment

SAME ARC = EQUAL
The rule
Angles at the circumference subtended by the same arc (in the same segment) are equal.
Exam phrase
"Angles in the same segment are equal."

4. Cyclic Quadrilateral

OPPOSITE = 180°
The rule
Opposite angles in a cyclic quadrilateral (all 4 vertices on the circle) add up to 180°.
Exam phrase
"Opposite angles in a cyclic quadrilateral sum to 180°."

5. Tangent & Radius

TANGENT ⊥ RADIUS
The rule
A tangent to a circle meets the radius at the point of contact at 90°.
Exam phrase
"A tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at the point of contact."

6. Two Tangents from a Point

EQUAL LENGTH
The rule
Two tangents drawn from an external point to a circle are equal in length.
Exam phrase
"Tangents from an external point are equal in length."

7. Alternate Segment Theorem

TANGENT & CHORD
The rule
The angle between a tangent and a chord at the point of contact equals the angle in the alternate segment.
Exam phrase
"Alternate segment theorem: the angle between a tangent and chord equals the angle in the alternate segment."

How to Spot Which Theorem

Can you see a tangent?
Tangent meets a radius at the circle? → Theorem 5 (tangent ⊥ radius = 90°)
Two tangents from an external point? → Theorem 6 (equal lengths)
Tangent meets a chord? → Theorem 7 (alternate segment)
Can you see the centre marked?
Angle at the centre AND angle at the circumference from the same arc? → Theorem 1 (centre = 2× circumference)
A diameter (line through centre) with a triangle on it? → Theorem 2 (angle in semicircle = 90°)
No centre, no tangent?
Two angles from the same chord, both on the same side? → Theorem 3 (same segment, equal angles)
A quadrilateral with all 4 vertices on the circle? → Theorem 4 (opposite angles = 180°)

Worked Example — Multi-Step Problem

Points A, B, C and D lie on a circle with centre O. Angle AOB = 104°. BD is a diameter. Find angle ACD.
Step 1 — Theorem 1 (angle at centre):
Angle AOB = 104° is at the centre. Angle ACB is at the circumference, subtended by the same arc AB.
So angle ACB = 104° ÷ 2 = 52°
Step 2 — Theorem 2 (angle in semicircle):
BD is a diameter, so angle BCD (the angle at C in the semicircle) = 90°
Step 3 — Put it together:
Angle ACD = angle BCD − angle ACB
Angle ACD = 90° − 52° = 38°

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Confusing "angle at centre" with other angles
The angle at the centre MUST have its vertex at point O. If the angle is at the circumference, it's not the centre angle.
Check: does the angle vertex sit on point O?
Not stating the theorem name
You MUST name the theorem to get the "reason" mark. Just writing the number isn't enough.
Always write: "because [theorem name]"
Not spotting a diameter
A diameter passes through the centre. If it's not through the centre, it's just a chord, not a diameter. No semicircle theorem.
A diameter MUST go through O!
Mixing up alternate segment
The angle between tangent and chord equals the angle in the OTHER segment, not the same one. Draw it out if unsure.
Tangent-chord angle = angle on the opposite side of the chord
