6 steps backed by cognitive science. No fluff.

1

Know what you don't know

Before you revise anything, RAG-rate every topic. Red = don't understand it. Amber = get the basics but wouldn't ace an exam question. Green = confident. Be brutally honest.
Science says: students waste up to 60% of revision time re-studying things they already know. RAG-rating forces you to spend time where it actually matters.
How: Go through the specification topic list. For each topic, ask: "Could I answer a 6-mark question on this right now?" If no → Red or Amber.
2

Learn it (briefly)

For Red topics only: read the material, watch a video, look at a crib sheet. Spend no more than 10 minutes understanding the concept. Don't take notes yet.
Science says: you need something in your head before you can retrieve it. But passive reading has diminishing returns after a few minutes. Get the gist, then move to step 3.
How: Use these revision cards, a textbook, or a short video. Focus on understanding the why, not memorising the what.
3

Test yourself (active recall)

Close the book. Cover the notes. Now write down everything you can remember about the topic from memory. Use a blank piece of paper or a whiteboard. No peeking.
Science says: the act of retrieving information from memory is what makes it stick. Every time you successfully recall something, the memory gets stronger. This is the single most effective revision technique known to research.
Ways to do it:
  • Brain dump: write everything you know about a topic on a blank page
  • Flashcards: read the question, say the answer BEFORE flipping
  • Past papers: do questions without notes, then mark yourself
  • Teach it: explain the topic out loud to someone (or an empty room)
4

Check and correct

Now open the book. Compare what you wrote with the actual material. Find the gaps — what did you miss? What did you get wrong? Mark those gaps clearly.
Science says: identifying errors is where the learning actually happens. The gap between what you thought you knew and what you actually know is the most valuable information in your revision.
How: Use a different colour pen to fill in what you missed. These gaps are your priority for next time. Update your RAG rating.
5

Space it out

Don't revise the same topic again today. Come back to it in 2-3 days. Then again in a week. Then again in two weeks. Each time, repeat steps 3 and 4.
Science says: spacing out your practice is dramatically more effective than massing it together. Forgetting a little bit between sessions is actually good — it makes the re-learning stronger. This is called the "spacing effect" and it's one of the most robust findings in all of psychology.
How: Use a revision timetable. Rotate subjects. Monday: Chemistry topics 1-2. Tuesday: Physics topics 1-2. Wednesday: Maths. Thursday: back to Chemistry topics 1-2 again. The gaps between sessions are doing the work.
6

Do past papers properly

In the final weeks, do full past papers under timed exam conditions. No notes. No phone. Exact time limit. Then mark them using the mark scheme. Study what you got wrong.
Science says: practising under exam conditions trains your brain to perform under pressure. It also reveals topic gaps you didn't know you had. The mark scheme teaches you exactly what examiners are looking for — often specific phrases and keywords.
How:
  • Find past papers on the exam board website (they're free)
  • Set a timer. No distractions. Do the whole paper
  • Mark it honestly using the mark scheme
  • For every wrong answer: understand WHY, then add that topic to your Red list

How Long? How Often?

25 min
Focus block
One topic, one task. Phone off. Full attention. When the timer goes, stop.
5 min
Break
Stand up. Move. Get water. Look at something far away. Don't check your phone.
3–4
Blocks per session
Then take a longer break (20–30 min). Two sessions per day max. Quality over quantity.

What a Good 25-Minute Block Looks Like

0–2 min
Quick recall: What do I already know about this topic? Jot it down from memory.
2–7 min
Fill gaps: Read/watch only the bits you couldn't recall. Don't re-read what you already know.
7–20 min
Practice: Do exam questions, flashcards, or a brain dump on this topic. No notes open.
20–23 min
Mark and correct: Check your answers. Note what you got wrong. Update your RAG rating.
23–25 min
Plan: Write down what to revisit next time. Then stop. Take your break.

RAG Rate Everything

Be honest. Green topics need the least time. Red topics need the most.
Red
Don't understand it. Couldn't answer a basic question. Need to learn from scratch.
Action: Learn → Test → Repeat in 2 days
Amber
Get the basics but would struggle with harder questions or multi-step problems.
Action: Practice questions → Repeat in 4 days
Green
Confident. Could explain it to someone else. Would get full marks on an exam question.
Action: Quick check once a week. Move on.
