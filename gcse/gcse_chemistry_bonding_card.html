Ionic Bonding

TRANSFER
What happens
Metal atoms lose electrons → become positive ions (cations).
Non-metal atoms gain electrons → become negative ions (anions).
Both get full outer shells (like noble gases).
Na+ transfers e− to Cl−
What holds it together
Strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions.
Ions pack into a giant ionic lattice — regular repeating 3D structure, billions of ions.
The sentence that gets full marks
"Strong electrostatic forces of attraction between oppositely charged ions in a giant ionic lattice."

Covalent Bonding

SHARING
What happens
Non-metal atoms share pairs of electrons so both get full outer shells.
Each shared pair = one covalent bond.
Double bond = two shared pairs. Triple bond = three.
H shares e− pair with H → H—H
Two types of covalent structure
Simple molecular (H2O, CO2, O2): small molecules, weak forces BETWEEN them.
Giant covalent (diamond, silicon dioxide): billions of atoms all bonded, no molecules.
The sentence that gets full marks
"A shared pair of electrons between two non-metal atoms."

Metallic Bonding

DELOCALISED
What happens
Metal atoms lose their outer electrons. These become delocalised (free to move).
What's left: a lattice of positive metal ions in a sea of delocalised electrons.
Cu+ Cu+ Cu+ Cu+
What holds it together
Strong electrostatic attraction between the positive ions and the sea of delocalised electrons.
This acts in all directions — very strong, needs lots of energy to break.
The sentence that gets full marks
"Strong electrostatic attraction between positive metal ions and a sea of delocalised electrons."

Simple Molecular

Small molecules, weak forces between them
Strong bonds WITHIN each molecule (covalent)
Weak forces BETWEEN molecules (intermolecular forces)
Low melting/boiling points (easy to separate molecules)
Don't conduct — no free electrons or ions
H₂O, CO₂, O₂, CH₄, NH₃

Giant Covalent

Billions of atoms, all bonded together
Strong bonds THROUGHOUT the entire structure
No weak forces — you must break covalent bonds to melt it
Very high melting points (takes huge energy)
Usually don't conduct (except graphite — delocalised electrons)
Diamond, graphite, silicon dioxide

Why don't ionic solids conduct?
Ions are in fixed positions in the lattice. They cannot move to carry charge. When melted or dissolved, ions are free to move, so they can carry charge → conducts.
Why do simple covalent substances have low boiling points?
The covalent bonds within molecules are strong, BUT the intermolecular forces between molecules are weak. It takes little energy to overcome these weak forces, so boiling point is low.
Why do metals conduct electricity?
Metals have delocalised electrons that are free to move through the structure. These electrons carry electrical charge when a voltage is applied.
Why are metals malleable?
The layers of ions can slide over each other without breaking the metallic bond. The sea of electrons shifts with the ions, holding the structure together in its new shape.
Why are ionic compounds brittle?
If layers shift, ions of the same charge line up next to each other. They repel, and the crystal shatters. Unlike metals, there's no "sea of electrons" to hold it together.
Why does diamond have a high melting point?
Diamond is a giant covalent structure. Each carbon is bonded to four others by strong covalent bonds. There are many bonds to break, requiring large amounts of energy.

How to answer the classic 6-mark bonding question

"Explain why sodium chloride has a high melting point but does not conduct electricity as a solid, whereas it does conduct when molten."
Structure: Sodium chloride has a giant ionic lattice structure. Na+ and Cl− ions are arranged in a regular pattern.
High melting point: There are strong electrostatic forces of attraction between the oppositely charged ions. A large amount of energy is needed to overcome these forces, so the melting point is high.
Doesn't conduct as solid: The ions are held in fixed positions in the lattice. They cannot move to carry electrical charge.
Conducts when molten: When melted, the ionic lattice breaks down. The ions are now free to move and can carry electrical charge, so it conducts.
