Distance-Time vs Velocity-Time — how to read them, what they mean, and how to calculate from them

2. How to Read the Gradient

Step 1 Pick two points on the straight part of the line.
Read off their coordinates: (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂).
Step 2 Draw a triangle — the vertical side is the rise (change in y), the horizontal side is the run (change in x).
Step 3 Calculate the gradient: gradient = rise ÷ run = (y₂ − y₁) ÷ (x₂ − x₁)
Worked Example — D-T Graph
Points: (2 s, 20 m) and (6 s, 60 m)
gradient = (60 − 20) ÷ (6 − 2) = 40 ÷ 4 = 10 m/s
The object is travelling at 10 m/s.
Worked Example — V-T Graph
Points: (0 s, 0 m/s) and (5 s, 15 m/s)
gradient = (15 − 0) ÷ (5 − 0) = 15 ÷ 5 = 3 m/s²
The object is accelerating at 3 m/s².

3. How to Calculate Area Under a V-T Graph

Rectangle
area = base × height
Constant speed: v × t
Triangle
area = ½ × base × height
Accelerating from zero
Compound
rect + triangle
Accelerating from a speed
Worked Example
A car travels at 10 m/s for 4 seconds, then accelerates uniformly to 20 m/s over the next 6 seconds.
Phase 1 (rectangle): distance = 10 × 4 = 40 m
Phase 2 (rectangle): distance = 10 × 6 = 60 m
Phase 2 (triangle): distance = ½ × 6 × 10 = 30 m
Total distance = 40 + 60 + 30 = 130 m

5. The Killer Mistakes

✘ Confusing speed with acceleration
On a D-T graph, gradient = speed.
On a V-T graph, gradient = acceleration.
They are NOT the same thing!
✘ Reading the wrong axis
Always check: is the y-axis distance or velocity?
This changes everything about how you read the graph.
✘ Forgetting area under V-T = distance
The area under a V-T graph gives you distance travelled.
NOT speed. NOT acceleration. Distance.
✘ Missing units in gradient calculations
D-T gradient: m ÷ s = m/s (speed)
V-T gradient: m/s ÷ s = m/s² (acceleration)
No units = lost marks.
