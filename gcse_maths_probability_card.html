Probability — The Complete Picture

AND / OR • Tree Diagrams • Venn Diagrams • Sample Spaces

1. The Two Rules — Learn These or Fail

"AND"
= MULTIPLY ×
P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
"this AND that" → times
"OR"
= ADD +
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)
"this OR that" → plus
The OR rule above is for mutually exclusive events (they can't both happen at once). When events CAN overlap, see the Venn Diagram section below for the full formula.

2. Tree Diagrams — With Replacement

How to draw one

  • Each branch represents one possible outcome
  • Write probabilities ON the branches
  • Outcomes are listed at the end of each path
  • Branches from the same point MUST add up to 1
  • Multiply ALONG branches (AND rule)
  • Add BETWEEN final outcomes (OR rule)

Example: Bag contains 3 red balls and 2 blue balls. Pick one, replace it, pick again.

  • 3/5 Red
    • 3/5 Red RR 3/5 × 3/5 = 9/25
    • 2/5 Blue RB 3/5 × 2/5 = 6/25
  • 2/5 Blue
    • 3/5 Red BR 2/5 × 3/5 = 6/25
    • 2/5 Blue BB 2/5 × 2/5 = 4/25
Worked calculation — P(at least one red)
Method 1 (OR rule): P(RR) + P(RB) + P(BR) = 9/25 + 6/25 + 6/25 = 21/25
Method 2 (shortcut): P(at least one red) = 1 − P(no red) = 1 − P(BB) = 1 − 4/25 = 21/25
P(at least one red) = 21/25 = 0.84
Check — all outcomes sum to 1
9/25 + 6/25 + 6/25 + 4/25 = 25/25 = 1 ✓

3. Without Replacement — The Fractions Change!

When you don't put the item back, the second set of probabilities CHANGE because one item is gone. The total goes from 5 → 4 and the count of whatever you picked goes down by 1.

This is what makes events dependent (also called conditional).

Same bag: 3 red, 2 blue. Pick one, DON'T replace, pick again.

  • 3/5 Red
    • 2/4 Red RR 3/5 × 2/4 = 6/20
    • 2/4 Blue RB 3/5 × 2/4 = 6/20
  • 2/5 Blue
    • 3/4 Red BR 2/5 × 3/4 = 6/20
    • 1/4 Blue BB 2/5 × 1/4 = 2/20
Key differences to spot
After picking Red first: 2 red left, 2 blue left → denominator is now 4 not 5
After picking Blue first: 3 red left, 1 blue left → denominator is now 4 not 5
Check: 6/20 + 6/20 + 6/20 + 2/20 = 20/20 = 1 ✓
Worked — P(both the same colour)
P(RR) + P(BB) = 6/20 + 2/20 = 8/20 = 2/5
P(same colour) = 2/5 = 0.4

4. Venn Diagrams

Reading the diagram (total = 50)

P(A) = (10 + 5)/50 = 15/50
P(B) = (5 + 15)/50 = 20/50
P(A ∩ B) = 5/50 = 5/50
P(A ∪ B) = (10 + 5 + 15)/50 = 30/50
P(neither) = 20/50 = 20/50
When events are NOT mutually exclusive:
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B)
Subtract the overlap or you count it twice!
Check with numbers
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B)
15/50 + 20/50 − 5/50 = 30/50 ✓ matches the diagram

5. Sample Space Diagrams

A grid showing every possible outcome of two events. Best for two dice, spinners, or coins.

When to use: when you have two independent events and want to see all combinations.

Example: Sum of two dice

+ 1 2 3 4 5 6
1 234567
2 345678
3 456789
4 5678910
5 67891011
6 789101112
Reading the grid
Total outcomes: 6 × 6 = 36
P(sum = 7): Count the highlighted 7s → 6 of them → 6/36 = 1/6
P(sum = 2): Only one way (1+1) → 1/36
P(sum > 10): Sums of 11 and 12 → 2 + 1 = 3 ways → 3/36 = 1/12

7 is the most likely sum. 2 and 12 are the least likely. That's why 7 matters in board games!

6. Expected Frequency

Expected frequency = probability × number of trials
How many times you EXPECT an outcome to happen
Example
P(heads) = 0.5. You flip a coin 200 times.
Expected heads = 0.5 × 200 = 100
Expect 100 heads
Another example
P(rolling a 6) = 1/6. You roll a die 300 times.
Expected sixes = 1/6 × 300 = 50
Expect 50 sixes

“Expected” does NOT mean “guaranteed.” You might get 97 or 108 heads — that's normal. Expected frequency is the long-run average, not a promise.

7. Common Mistakes — Don't Be That Student

  • Adding when you should multiply. "A AND B" means multiply. If you see "and" and reach for the + sign, stop. AND = ×.
  • Forgetting to change probabilities for "without replacement." If one ball is taken out, the total goes DOWN by 1 and the colour count changes. The second branch fractions are DIFFERENT from the first.
  • Forgetting the complement shortcut: P(not A) = 1 − P(A). "At least one" questions are almost always easier using 1 − P(none).
  • Probabilities must be between 0 and 1. If your answer is negative or greater than 1, something went wrong. Go back and check.
  • All outcomes must sum to 1. In a tree diagram, add up every final branch. If they don't total 1, you've made an error somewhere.
  • Double-counting in Venn diagrams. P(A or B) ≠ P(A) + P(B) unless the events are mutually exclusive. If there's an overlap, subtract P(A and B).
  • Confusing "at least one" with "exactly one." "At least one red" includes RR, RB, and BR. "Exactly one red" is only RB and BR.
