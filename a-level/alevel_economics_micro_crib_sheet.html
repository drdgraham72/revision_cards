A-Level Microeconomics — Year 12 Crib Sheet

Edexcel Theme 1 · Markets & Market Failure
The Economic Problem 1.1
Scarcity
Scarcity: unlimited wants but limited resources — the fundamental economic problem. Forces societies to make choices about allocation.
Choice & Opportunity Cost
Opportunity cost: the next best alternative forgone when a choice is made.
Not all alternatives — only the single best one given up. Applies to individuals, firms, and governments.
ExampleGovernment spends £10bn on defence → opportunity cost = £10bn of NHS spending (if that was next best use)
PPF (Production Possibility Frontier)
Shows maximum output combinations of two goods using all resources efficiently.
Key pointsOn the curve = productive efficiency. Inside = inefficiency / unemployed resources. Outside = currently unattainable.
Shifts outward: improved technology, more resources, better education/training, immigration. Shifts inward: natural disaster, war, emigration. Movement along = reallocation (shows opportunity cost as a trade-off).
Common mistake: saying points outside the PPF are "impossible" — they are unattainable with current resources but achievable with economic growth.
Specialisation & Division of Labour
Specialisation: concentrating on producing goods/services where you have lowest opportunity cost. Division of labour: breaking production into separate tasks.
BenefitsHigher productivity, economies of scale, higher output, skill development, time saving
DrawbacksWorker boredom, structural unemployment if demand changes, over-dependence on others, monotony reduces quality
Economic Systems
Free market: resources allocated by price mechanism; private ownership; profit motive. Command: state allocates resources; government ownership; central planning. Mixed: combination of market and government allocation (all real-world economies).
Evaluation: no economy is purely free market or purely command. The debate is about the degree of government intervention, not whether it should exist at all.
Demand 1.2
Law of Demand
As price rises, quantity demanded falls (ceteris paribus) — inverse relationship. The demand curve slopes downward left to right.
Movement vs Shift
Movement along: caused by a change in the good's own price. Shift of the whole curve: caused by non-price factors (conditions of demand).
Demand Curve Shifters
Income: normal goods — demand rises with income (shift right); inferior goods — demand falls with income (shift left). Substitutes: price of substitute rises → demand for this good rises (shift right). Complements: price of complement rises → demand for this good falls (shift left). Tastes/fashion: favourable change → shift right. Population: larger population → shift right. Advertising: successful campaign → shift right. Expectations: expected future price rise → demand rises now.
Common mistake: confusing a movement along the demand curve (price change) with a shift (non-price factor). Examiners penalise this heavily.
Supply 1.2
Law of Supply
As price rises, quantity supplied rises (ceteris paribus) — positive relationship. The supply curve slopes upward left to right.
Supply Curve Shifters
Costs of production: wages, raw materials rise → shift left (decrease in supply). Technology: improved tech → shift right (lower costs per unit). Subsidies: government subsidy → shift right (reduces costs). Indirect taxes: tax imposed → shift left (increases costs). Weather/natural factors: good harvest → shift right for agricultural goods. Number of firms: more firms enter → market supply shifts right.
Market Equilibrium & Price Mechanism 1.2
Equilibrium
Market equilibrium: where quantity demanded = quantity supplied (S=D). The equilibrium price clears the market — no excess supply or excess demand.
Excess supply (price above equilibrium): firms cut price → movement down supply, up demand → back to equilibrium. Excess demand (price below equilibrium): firms raise price → movement up supply, down demand → back to equilibrium.
Price Mechanism Functions
Rationing: prices rise to ration scarce goods among those willing and able to pay. Signalling: prices signal information to producers and consumers about where resources are needed. Incentive: high prices incentivise firms to supply more (profit motive) and consumers to demand less.
A* evaluation: the price mechanism assumes perfect information, rational behaviour, and no externalities. Where these fail, we get market failure — linking 1.2 to 1.3/1.4.
Consumer & Producer Surplus
Consumer surplus: difference between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay. Area below demand curve, above price, to the left of Q. Producer surplus: difference between the price received and the minimum price producers are willing to accept. Area above supply curve, below price, to the left of Q. Total welfare = CS + PS. Maximised at free market equilibrium (allocative efficiency).
In diagrams: CS is the triangle above price line, below demand curve. PS is the triangle below price line, above supply curve. Always label these clearly.
Elasticity — PED 1.2
PED Formula
PED = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in price
% change = (new − old) ÷ old × 100
PED is always negative (law of demand) but we use the absolute value.
Elastic: |PED| > 1 (demand responsive to price). Inelastic: |PED| < 1 (demand unresponsive). Unitary: |PED| = 1.
Perfectly elastic: PED = infinity (horizontal demand curve — any price rise = zero demand). Perfectly inelastic: PED = 0 (vertical demand curve — demand unchanged whatever the price).
PED Determinants
Substitutes: more substitutes → more elastic. Necessity vs luxury: necessities → inelastic; luxuries → elastic. Proportion of income: larger share of income → more elastic. Time: longer time period → more elastic (more time to find alternatives). Habit/addiction: addictive goods → inelastic.
PED & Total Revenue
Total Revenue = Price × Quantity
If demand is elastic: price rise → TR falls (quantity falls proportionally more). Price cut → TR rises. If demand is inelastic: price rise → TR rises (quantity falls proportionally less). Price cut → TR falls.
A* point: firms with inelastic demand (e.g. Apple) can raise prices and increase revenue. This explains why governments tax inelastic goods (tobacco, petrol) — revenue is high and quantity doesn't fall much.
Elasticity — YED, XED, PES 1.2
YED (Income Elasticity)
YED = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in income
Normal goods: YED > 0 (demand rises with income). Inferior goods: YED < 0 (demand falls as income rises).
Necessities: 0 < YED < 1 (income inelastic — demand rises but less than proportionally). Luxuries: YED > 1 (income elastic — demand rises more than proportionally).
A* application: as economies grow, demand for luxury goods rises faster than necessities. Firms selling inferior goods (e.g. budget brands) see falling demand in booms — relevant for business strategy.
XED (Cross Elasticity)
XED = % change in Qd of good A ÷ % change in price of good B
Substitutes: XED > 0 (positive — price of B rises, demand for A rises). Complements: XED < 0 (negative — price of B rises, demand for A falls).
Unrelated goods: XED = 0 or close to 0. The higher the value, the stronger the relationship between goods.
Don't confuse the direction: XED measures the effect of good B's price on good A's quantity demanded, not the other way round.
PES (Price Elasticity of Supply)
PES = % change in quantity supplied ÷ % change in price
PES is always positive (law of supply). Determinants: Time: longer time → more elastic (firms can adjust capacity). Spare capacity: more spare capacity → more elastic (can increase output easily). Stocks/inventories: more stocks → more elastic. Mobility of factors: easier to switch resources → more elastic. Perishability: perishable goods → inelastic supply.
Market Failure 1.3
Definition
Market failure: where the free market mechanism fails to achieve allocative efficiency — resources are misallocated, leading to a net welfare loss.
Externalities
Occur when production or consumption affects third parties not involved in the transaction.
Negative externality of productionFactory pollution → MSC > MPC. Overproduction. Welfare loss triangle between MSC and MPC. E.g. carbon emissions from manufacturing.
Negative externality of consumptionSmoking, alcohol → MSB < MPB. Overconsumption. E.g. passive smoking harms others.
Positive externality of productionR&D spillovers → MSC < MPC. Underproduction. E.g. tech firm research benefits other industries.
Positive externality of consumptionEducation, vaccination → MSB > MPB. Underconsumption. E.g. educated workforce benefits whole economy.
MSC = MPC + external cost
MSB = MPB + external benefit
Always state whether the externality is in production or consumption — examiners require this distinction. Also: the welfare loss is the triangle between the social and private curves, not the whole area.
Merit & Demerit Goods
Merit goods: goods that are underconsumed because consumers undervalue the benefits (positive externalities + information failure). E.g. education, healthcare, museums. Demerit goods: goods that are overconsumed because consumers undervalue the costs (negative externalities + information failure). E.g. tobacco, alcohol, gambling.
A* point: merit/demerit goods involve BOTH externalities AND information failure. Many students only mention one — you need both for full marks.
Public Goods
Non-rival: one person's use does not reduce availability for others. Non-excludable: impossible to prevent non-payers from consuming.
Free rider problem: rational consumers won't pay if they can't be excluded → no private firm will provide → market fails completely (missing market). E.g. street lighting, national defence, flood defences. Quasi-public goods: have some but not all characteristics (e.g. roads — non-rival until congestion).
Information Gaps
Asymmetric information: one party in a transaction has more/better information than the other. Imperfect information: consumers or producers lack full knowledge. E.g. second-hand cars (seller knows faults, buyer doesn't), health risks of smoking, financial products. Leads to: adverse selection, moral hazard, over/underconsumption.
MSC/MSB Diagrams
Allocative efficiency: where MSB = MSC (socially optimal output). Free market produces: where MPB = MPC (privately optimal output). The gap between social and private optimum = welfare loss (deadweight loss triangle). For negative externalities: free market overproduces. For positive externalities: free market underproduces.
Always label: MPC, MSC, MPB, MSB, free market output (Qm), socially optimal output (Qs), welfare loss triangle. Missing labels lose marks.
Government Intervention 1.4
Indirect Taxes
Specific tax: fixed amount per unit (shifts supply left by fixed distance, parallel). Ad valorem tax: percentage of price (supply pivots — gap widens at higher prices). E.g. VAT at 20%.
Tax incidence depends on PED/PES: inelastic demand → consumer bears more; elastic demand → producer bears more.
Evaluation: effective for inelastic goods (tobacco); may be regressive (hit low-income harder); may encourage black markets; revenue can fund alternatives.
Subsidies
Government payment to producers → shifts supply right (reduces costs). Increases output, reduces price paid by consumers. Used for merit goods, positive externalities.
Subsidy drawbacks: opportunity cost of government spending, may create dependency, difficult to calculate correct amount, may benefit producers more than consumers.
Maximum Prices (Price Ceilings)
Set below equilibrium to make goods affordable. Creates excess demand (shortage). E.g. rent controls. Effects: shortages, black markets, reduced quality, need for rationing (queuing/lottery).
A maximum price set ABOVE equilibrium has no effect — the market clears below it anyway.
Minimum Prices (Price Floors)
Set above equilibrium to protect producers/workers. Creates excess supply. E.g. National Minimum Wage, agricultural price floors. Effects: surplus, government may need to buy up excess, may cause unemployment (if applied to labour market).
Regulation & Legislation
Laws to ban/restrict activities. E.g. pollution limits, age restrictions on alcohol, planning regulations. Advantages: directly addresses market failure, legally enforceable. Disadvantages: costly to enforce, may be evaded, may not address root cause.
Provision of Public Goods
Government directly provides goods the market won't (non-rival, non-excludable). Funded through taxation. E.g. defence, street lighting, judiciary.
Information Provision
Government campaigns to correct information failure. E.g. nutritional labelling, health warnings on cigarettes, energy efficiency ratings.
Often used alongside other policies. Low cost but effectiveness depends on whether consumers act on the information — behavioural economics suggests they often don't.
Government Failure
Government failure: where government intervention leads to a net welfare loss or makes the misallocation of resources worse than the market failure it aimed to correct.
Causes: unintended consequences (e.g. minimum wage → unemployment), administrative costs exceeding benefits, distortion of market signals, political short-termism, regulatory capture, imperfect information by policymakers.
A* evaluation: always weigh intervention against government failure. The question is not "does market failure exist?" but "will intervention improve the outcome?" — this is the key to top-band answers.
