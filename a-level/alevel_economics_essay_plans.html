Every essay follows this structure

Para 1
Define
Key terms. Set the context. 2-3 sentences.
Para 2-3
Analyse
Point → chain of reasoning → diagram → link back to question.
Para 4-5
Counter
The other side. "However..." or a second analytical point.
Para 6
Evaluate
Depends on... Short run vs long run. Magnitude. Assumptions.
Para 7
Conclude
Weigh up. Make a judgement. "On balance..." Don't sit on the fence.

Microeconomics
"Evaluate the view that government intervention is always necessary to correct market failure." 25 marks ▼
Define & Context
Define market failure (where the free market fails to allocate resources efficiently) and government intervention (taxes, subsidies, regulation, provision).
Analysis 1: Yes, intervention is needed
Negative externalities — production/consumption creates costs to third parties (e.g. pollution). Overproduction at free market → MSC > MPC → welfare loss → tax shifts MPC to MSC → socially optimal output Diagram: MSC/MPC with welfare loss triangle
Public goods — non-rival, non-excludable → free rider problem → market won't provide → government must.
Analysis 2: Behavioural justification
Bounded rationality — consumers don't always act rationally (demerit goods, info gaps). Consumers overconsume harmful goods → nudges/regulation can correct → e.g. sugar tax, plain packaging
Counter: Government failure
However, intervention can cause government failure:
  • Information gaps — government may not know the correct level of tax/subsidy
  • Unintended consequences — e.g. rent controls causing housing shortages
  • Administrative costs — may exceed the welfare gain
  • Political self-interest — decisions driven by votes not efficiency
Evaluation
It depends on: the type of market failure (public goods REQUIRE intervention, externalities might self-correct via Coase theorem), the magnitude of the failure vs the cost of intervention, and whether the market can develop private solutions (e.g. carbon trading).
Conclusion
Not always — for public goods, yes. For externalities, the optimal level of intervention depends on whether government can accurately identify the externality and set the right level of correction. The word "always" makes the statement too strong.
Define: market failure, allocative efficiency, externality, government failure, Coase theorem
"Evaluate the impact of imposing an indirect tax on a demerit good." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define indirect tax (tax on spending, shifts supply left) and demerit good (overconsumed, negative externalities or information failure).
Analysis 1: How it works
Tax → supply shifts left (S to S+tax) → price rises → quantity falls → consumption closer to socially optimal level Diagram: S/D with tax shifting S left, showing new equilibrium
Tax revenue can fund NHS/education → double dividend.
Internalises the externality — polluter pays principle.
Counter & Evaluation
However:
  • PED matters: if demand is inelastic (e.g. cigarettes), quantity barely falls — tax raises revenue but doesn't change behaviour much
  • Regressive: hits low-income consumers hardest (higher % of income)
  • Black market: high tax may push consumers to illegal alternatives
  • Information problem: government may set tax too high or too low vs the externality
  • Time lags: behaviour change takes time, especially with addictive goods
Conclusion
Effective when demand is relatively elastic and combined with other policies (education, regulation). Less effective for addictive goods where demand is inelastic. The optimal tax rate = the marginal external cost, but this is hard to calculate precisely.
Define: indirect tax, demerit good, PED, incidence of tax, deadweight loss, regressive
"To what extent does monopoly lead to a worse outcome for consumers than perfect competition?" 25 marks ▼
Define
Define monopoly (single seller, 25%+ market share, price maker) and perfect competition (many sellers, homogeneous product, price taker).
Analysis 1: Monopoly is worse
Monopoly restricts output → higher price, lower quantity → allocative inefficiency (P > MC) → deadweight loss → consumer surplus falls Diagram: Monopoly MC/MR/AR with deadweight loss vs PC P=MC
X-inefficiency: no competitive pressure → costs may be higher than necessary.
Supernormal profit in long run (barriers to entry protect it).
Counter: Monopoly can be better
However:
  • Economies of scale: monopoly may achieve lower AC than many small firms → could pass on lower prices (natural monopoly argument)
  • Dynamic efficiency: supernormal profit funds R&D → innovation → better products (Schumpeter)
  • Cross-subsidisation: profits from one product can fund loss-making services
  • Perfect competition is theoretical: rarely exists in practice — monopolistic competition is more realistic comparator
Conclusion
Depends on whether the monopoly is contestable (threat of entry disciplines behaviour), whether it exploits its position or invests in innovation, and whether regulation (e.g. CMA, price caps) keeps it in check. A regulated natural monopoly may be the best outcome for consumers.
Define: allocative efficiency, productive efficiency, dynamic efficiency, X-inefficiency, natural monopoly, contestability
"Evaluate the impact of a national minimum wage on the labour market." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define national minimum wage (price floor in labour market, set above equilibrium wage). Distinguish NMW from National Living Wage.
Analysis 1: Competitive labour market
NMW above equilibrium → supply of labour > demand → surplus of workers → unemployment → disequilibrium Diagram: S/D for labour with NMW above We, showing excess supply
Higher costs for firms → may reduce hiring, cut hours, or raise prices.
Analysis 2: Monopsony employer
However, if employer is a monopsony: Monopsony pays below competitive wage → NMW can increase BOTH wages AND employment → moves towards competitive outcome Diagram: Monopsony with MCL above ACL, NMW between
Evaluation
  • Depends on how far above equilibrium: small increase = minimal unemployment; large increase = significant job losses
  • Sector matters: low-skill sectors more affected than high-skill
  • Multiplier effect: low-paid workers have high MPC → spending increases AD
  • Reduces inequality and in-work poverty, but may not help the unemployed
  • Firms may absorb costs through lower profits rather than cutting jobs
Conclusion
In a monopsony labour market, a moderate NMW can increase employment. In a competitive market, it creates unemployment. The real-world impact is somewhere between — empirical evidence (e.g. Card & Krueger) suggests moderate minimum wages have small negative effects on employment. The level relative to the median wage matters most.
Define: NMW, monopsony, MRP, price floor, derived demand, wage elasticity of demand
Macroeconomics
"Evaluate the effectiveness of fiscal policy in reducing unemployment." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define fiscal policy (government spending and taxation to influence AD) and unemployment (those willing and able to work but without a job).
Analysis 1: How it reduces demand-deficient unemployment
Increase G or cut T → AD shifts right → real GDP rises → derived demand for labour rises → unemployment falls Diagram: AD/AS with AD shifting right along Keynesian LRAS
Multiplier effect: initial injection is respent → total increase in GDP is greater than the initial change. Multiplier = 1/(1-MPC).
Counter & Evaluation
  • Type of unemployment matters: fiscal policy works for cyclical/demand-deficient, NOT for structural (need supply-side policies instead)
  • Time lags: planning, implementation, and impact can take 12-18 months
  • Crowding out: government borrowing may raise interest rates → reduces private investment
  • Budget deficit: expansionary fiscal policy increases national debt
  • Inflation risk: if economy is near full capacity, AD shift causes demand-pull inflation not lower unemployment
  • Multiplier size: depends on MPC, leakages (tax, imports, savings) — may be small in open economy
Conclusion
Effective for demand-deficient unemployment in a recession with spare capacity. Less effective near full employment or for structural unemployment. Best used alongside supply-side policies for a comprehensive approach. Keynesian view: essential. Classical view: only short-term, creates long-term debt problems.
Define: fiscal policy, multiplier, MPC, crowding out, demand-deficient unemployment, structural unemployment, output gap
"Evaluate the effectiveness of monetary policy in controlling inflation." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define monetary policy (use of interest rates, money supply, QE by central bank) and inflation (sustained rise in general price level, measured by CPI).
Analysis 1: Raising interest rates
Bank rate rises → cost of borrowing rises → consumption & investment fall → AD shifts left → inflationary pressure reduced Diagram: AD/AS with AD shifting left
Also: higher rates attract hot money → exchange rate appreciates → imports cheaper → reduces cost-push inflation.
Savings more attractive → consumption falls further.
Counter & Evaluation
  • Type of inflation: effective for demand-pull, but NOT cost-push (raising rates won't fix oil price shocks)
  • Time lags: 18-24 months for full transmission
  • Blunt instrument: affects ALL sectors, not just overheating ones
  • Liquidity trap: if rates are already near zero (like post-2008), can't cut further
  • QE effectiveness: may inflate asset prices rather than real economic activity
  • Mortgage holders: rate rises disproportionately affect variable-rate mortgage borrowers
Conclusion
Effective for demand-pull inflation when there is room to adjust rates. Less effective for cost-push or at the zero lower bound. Works best when combined with fiscal restraint and clear central bank communication (managing expectations).
Define: base rate, MPC, CPI, transmission mechanism, quantitative easing, liquidity trap, hot money
"Evaluate the view that supply-side policies are the most effective way to achieve economic growth." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define supply-side policies (policies to increase productive capacity / shift LRAS right) and economic growth (increase in real GDP over time).
Analysis 1: Supply-side strengths
Education/training → more productive workforce → LRAS shifts right → sustained growth without inflation Diagram: AD/AS with LRAS shifting right, showing growth at stable prices
Deregulation → lower business costs → more firms enter → competition → efficiency.
Tax cuts on profits → incentive to invest → capital deepening → productivity gains.
Key advantage: non-inflationary growth (capacity expands alongside demand).
Counter: Limitations
  • Very long time lags: education takes years; infrastructure takes decades
  • Costly: government spending on education/infrastructure is expensive
  • Market-based may increase inequality: deregulation and tax cuts may favour the wealthy
  • Won't help in a recession: if AD is deficient, expanding capacity doesn't help — need demand-side stimulus first
  • No guarantee: cutting corporation tax doesn't guarantee firms will invest (may just increase dividends)
Conclusion
"Most effective" is too strong. Supply-side policies are essential for long-run sustainable growth, but in the short run, demand-side policies may be needed to close the output gap. Best approach: both together — fiscal/monetary stimulus for short-run recovery, supply-side for long-run capacity.
Define: LRAS, productive capacity, human capital, labour productivity, output gap, Laffer curve
Business & Labour Market
"Evaluate whether consumers benefit from oligopolistic market structures." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define oligopoly (few dominant firms, high concentration ratio, interdependent decision-making, barriers to entry).
Analysis 1: Consumers lose
Collusion (tacit or overt) → firms act as a monopoly → restrict output, raise prices → allocative inefficiency, lower consumer surplus. Diagram: Kinked demand curve or cartel diagram (joint profit max at MC=MR)
Price rigidity — kinked demand curve means prices don't fall even when costs do.
Counter: Consumers benefit
  • Non-price competition: quality, branding, innovation, customer service — consumers get better products
  • Economies of scale: large firms achieve lower AC, potentially lower prices than many small firms
  • Price wars: occasional breakdowns in collusion benefit consumers (e.g. supermarket price matching)
  • Dynamic efficiency: supernormal profits fund R&D (e.g. tech, pharmaceuticals)
Conclusion
Depends on whether firms compete or collude. If contestable (low barriers), competitive behaviour dominates. If collusive, consumers lose. Competition policy (CMA) is crucial in ensuring oligopolies behave competitively.
Define: oligopoly, interdependence, concentration ratio, kinked demand, game theory, Nash equilibrium, contestability
"Evaluate the view that profit maximisation is always the primary objective of firms." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define profit maximisation (output where MC = MR, maximum difference between TR and TC).
Analysis 1: Yes, it's primary
In perfect competition, firms MUST profit maximise to survive (only normal profit in LR).
Shareholders demand maximum returns → pressure on managers.
In the long run, all firms must at least cover costs or exit the market. Diagram: MC = MR profit maximisation
Counter: Alternative objectives
  • Revenue maximisation (MR = 0): managers may prefer growth/market share over profit (Baumol)
  • Sales maximisation (AC = AR): maximise output while breaking even
  • Satisficing: earn "good enough" profit while pursuing other goals (Cyert & March, principal-agent problem)
  • Social enterprise: some firms prioritise social/environmental goals
  • Predatory pricing: short-run losses to drive out competitors
Conclusion
Depends on ownership structure (shareholder-owned vs mutual vs public sector), market structure (competitive pressure forces profit max), and time horizon (short-run market share vs long-run profit). In practice, most large firms satisfice rather than strictly maximise.
Define: normal/supernormal profit, MC=MR, revenue maximisation, satisficing, principal-agent problem, divorce of ownership and control
"Evaluate whether economies of scale always lead to lower prices for consumers." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define economies of scale (falling long-run average costs as output increases). Internal (technical, managerial, financial, marketing, risk-bearing) vs external.
Analysis 1: Yes, lower prices
EoS → lower AC → firm can reduce prices and still make profit → consumer benefit Diagram: LRAC curve showing falling AC as output rises
In competitive markets, firms MUST pass on cost savings to retain market share.
Natural monopoly: one large firm has lower AC than multiple small firms → potentially lower prices for all.
Counter: Not necessarily
  • Market power: large firm may retain savings as supernormal profit rather than cutting prices
  • Diseconomies of scale: beyond MES, costs may RISE (coordination problems, bureaucracy)
  • X-inefficiency: lack of competition leads to waste/complacency
  • Price discrimination: monopolist may charge different consumers different prices
Conclusion
EoS can but don't always lead to lower prices. The key factor is market structure and contestability. In competitive/contestable markets, cost savings are more likely to be passed on. In monopolistic markets, regulation may be needed to ensure consumers benefit.
Define: EoS, MES, LRAC, natural monopoly, X-inefficiency, diseconomies of scale, contestability
The Global Economy
"Evaluate the impact of globalisation on developing economies." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define globalisation (increasing integration and interdependence of world economies through trade, capital flows, migration, technology).
Analysis 1: Benefits
FDI inflows → capital investment → jobs created → wages rise → GDP per capita increases → poverty reduction Access to global markets → export-led growth (e.g. China, Vietnam).
Technology transfer from TNCs → productivity gains.
Comparative advantage exploitation → specialisation → efficiency.
Counter: Costs
  • Inequality: benefits concentrated in urban areas/skilled workers — rural poverty may persist
  • Environmental degradation: "race to the bottom" in environmental standards
  • Dependency: reliance on TNCs who may relocate for cheaper labour
  • Terms of trade: developing countries often export low-value primary commodities
  • Cultural erosion: homogenisation of culture by Western TNCs
  • Profit repatriation: TNC profits flow back to home country, not reinvested locally
Conclusion
Globalisation has lifted millions out of poverty but the benefits are unevenly distributed. Success depends on governance quality, institutional strength, and whether the country can move up the value chain. Managed globalisation (with investment in education and infrastructure) is more beneficial than unrestricted openness.
Define: globalisation, FDI, TNC, comparative advantage, terms of trade, HDI, dependency
"Evaluate the view that protectionism is never justified." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define protectionism (government policies to restrict imports — tariffs, quotas, subsidies, embargoes, administrative barriers).
Analysis 1: Free trade is better (protectionism is bad)
Tariffs → higher import prices → consumers pay more → lower consumer surplus → allocative inefficiency Diagram: Tariff diagram showing welfare loss triangles
Comparative advantage theory: free trade allows specialisation → higher global output.
Protectionism invites retaliation → trade wars → everyone worse off.
Reduces competition → domestic firms become inefficient.
Counter: Sometimes justified
  • Infant industry argument: new industries need temporary protection to achieve EoS before competing globally
  • National security: strategic industries (defence, food, energy) shouldn't depend on imports
  • Anti-dumping: foreign firms selling below cost to destroy domestic competition
  • Protecting jobs: short-run political justification, especially in declining industries
  • Correcting trade imbalances: persistent current account deficit
Conclusion
"Never justified" is too absolute. Temporary, targeted protectionism (infant industry, anti-dumping) can be justified. But permanent, broad protectionism reduces efficiency and harms consumers. The key test: does the long-run benefit outweigh the short-run cost? Usually, free trade wins in the long run.
Define: tariff, quota, comparative advantage, infant industry, dumping, WTO, trade creation/diversion
"Evaluate policies to correct a persistent current account deficit." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define current account deficit (imports of goods/services/income exceed exports). Distinguish from budget deficit.
Policy 1: Expenditure-reducing
Raise interest rates or cut G → AD falls → income falls → import spending falls → current account improves But: causes unemployment and lower growth — painful trade-off.
Policy 2: Expenditure-switching
Depreciate exchange rate → imports dearer, exports cheaper → demand switches to domestic goods → current account improves But: only works if Marshall-Lerner condition is met (PED exports + PED imports > 1). J-curve effect: worsens in short run before improving. Diagram: J-curve showing initial worsening then improvement
Policy 3: Supply-side
Improve competitiveness: invest in education, R&D, infrastructure → more competitive exports → long-run improvement.
But: very long time lags. Doesn't help in the short run.
Conclusion
No single policy is sufficient. Short run: expenditure-switching can work if Marshall-Lerner holds. Long run: supply-side improvements in competitiveness are essential. The best approach depends on the cause of the deficit — if structural (uncompetitive economy), supply-side is crucial; if cyclical (temporary demand surge), it may self-correct.
Define: current account, Marshall-Lerner, J-curve, exchange rate, expenditure-reducing, expenditure-switching
"Evaluate the view that a floating exchange rate is always preferable to a fixed exchange rate." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define floating exchange rate (determined by market forces of supply and demand for the currency) and fixed exchange rate (pegged to another currency by the central bank).
Analysis 1: Floating advantages
  • Automatic adjustment: current account deficit → currency depreciates → exports cheaper → self-correcting
  • Monetary policy freedom: can set interest rates for domestic economy (not to defend the peg)
  • No need for reserves: central bank doesn't need to hold large foreign currency reserves
Counter: Fixed advantages
  • Certainty: businesses can plan trade/investment without exchange rate risk
  • Low inflation discipline: pegging to a low-inflation currency imports credibility
  • Prevents speculation: no room for destabilising currency speculation
  • But: requires large reserves, loses monetary independence, risk of speculative attacks if overvalued
Conclusion
Neither is "always" preferable. Floating suits large, diversified economies (UK, US). Fixed can suit small, open, inflation-prone economies. Many countries use a managed float — a pragmatic middle ground. "Always" makes the statement too strong.
Define: floating, fixed, managed float, hot money, speculation, foreign currency reserves, interest rate parity
"Evaluate strategies to promote economic development in developing countries." 25 marks ▼
Define
Define economic development (improvement in living standards, health, education, freedom — broader than GDP growth). Distinguish from economic growth. Mention HDI.
Strategy 1: Trade liberalisation
Remove trade barriers → exploit comparative advantage → export-led growth → FDI inflows → job creation → development But: may benefit only certain sectors/regions; terms of trade may deteriorate; infant industries may be destroyed.
Strategy 2: Foreign aid
Aid can fund infrastructure, education, healthcare directly.
But: may create dependency, corruption may divert funds, tied aid benefits donor country, may distort markets.
Strategy 3: Microfinance & debt relief
Microfinance: small loans to entrepreneurs → self-employment → income generation (e.g. Grameen Bank).
Debt relief: freed-up government revenue can be spent on public services.
But: microfinance has high interest rates; debt relief may encourage future reckless borrowing.
Conclusion
No single strategy is sufficient. Development requires a combination: trade access, institutional reform (rule of law, property rights, anti-corruption), investment in human capital, and appropriate levels of aid. The most successful developing countries (e.g. South Korea, Botswana) used managed trade + strong institutions + investment in education.
Define: economic development, HDI, FDI, Harrod-Domar model, microfinance, tied aid, debt relief, institutional quality
