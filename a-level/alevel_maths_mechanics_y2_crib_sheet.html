Further Kinematics (Calculus) Ch 6
Calculus relationships
For motion with variable acceleration, the suvat equations do not apply. Use calculus instead:
v = ds/dt  (differentiate displacement)
a = dv/dt = d²s/dt²  (differentiate velocity)
s = ∫v dt  (integrate velocity)
v = ∫a dt  (integrate acceleration)
Vector form (i, j)
Position, velocity and acceleration can be given as vectors:
r = xi + yj
v = ẋi + ẏj = dr/dt
a = ẍi + ÿj = dv/dt
Differentiate and integrate each component separately.
Finding displacement
Integrate velocity and use initial conditions to find the constant of integration:
s = ∫v dt + C
Use s(0) = s₀ to find C
ExampleIf v = 3t² − 2t + 1 and s = 4 when t = 0:
s = t³ − t² + t + C → 4 = 0 + C → s = t³ − t² + t + 4
Maximum / minimum
Maximum displacement: set v = ds/dt = 0 and check the sign changes.
Maximum velocity: set a = dv/dt = 0 and verify it is a maximum using d²v/dt².
At rest: v = 0
At max speed: a = 0 (and a changes sign)
Speed & magnitude
Speed is the magnitude of velocity:
|v| = √(ẋ² + ẏ²)
Distance from origin: |r| = √(x² + y²)
When integrating to find displacement or velocity, you MUST include the constant of integration and use initial conditions to find it. Forgetting +C is a guaranteed mark loss.
If the question says "variable acceleration" or gives acceleration as a function of t, that is your cue to use calculus, not suvat. Read carefully — suvat only works for constant acceleration.
Projectiles (Advanced) Ch 7
Resolving at angle θ
For a projectile launched at speed u at angle θ to the horizontal:
Horizontal: u_x = u cos θ  (constant, no acceleration)
Vertical: u_y = u sin θ  (acceleration = −g)
Key equations
Horizontal: x = (u cos θ)t
Vertical: y = (u sin θ)t − ½gt²
Vertical velocity: v_y = u sin θ − gt
Treat horizontal and vertical components independently. There is no horizontal acceleration (ignoring air resistance).
Maximum height
At the highest point, the vertical velocity is zero:
v_y = 0 → t = (u sin θ)/g
Max height H = u²sin²θ / (2g)
Time of flight
Time for the projectile to return to the same vertical level:
T = 2u sin θ / g
This is twice the time to reach maximum height (by symmetry).
Range
Horizontal distance when the projectile returns to launch height:
R = u²sin 2θ / g
Maximum range occurs when θ = 45°.
Trajectory equation
Eliminate t to get y as a function of x:
y = x tan θ − gx² / (2u²cos²θ)
Useful when you need to find the height at a given horizontal distance, or vice versa.
Always resolve initial velocity into components FIRST. Using the full speed u in vertical equations is the most common projectile error. Horizontal: u cos θ. Vertical: u sin θ.
At maximum height, only v_y = 0. The horizontal velocity u cos θ never changes. The speed at the top is u cos θ, not zero (unless launched vertically).
Elastic Strings & Springs Ch 10
Hooke's law
The tension in an elastic string or spring is proportional to the extension:
T = kx = λx / l

k = spring constant (stiffness)
λ = modulus of elasticity
l = natural length
x = extension (or compression for springs)
Strings can only be in tension (T ≥ 0). Springs can be in tension or compression.
Elastic potential energy
EPE = ½kx² = λx² / (2l)
This is the energy stored in a stretched (or compressed) elastic string or spring. Used in energy conservation problems.
Energy conservation
For problems involving elastic strings/springs, apply conservation of energy:
KE₁ + PE₁ + EPE₁ = KE₂ + PE₂ + EPE₂

½mv² + mgh + ½kx² = constant (if no external forces do work)
Beyond natural length
A string becomes slack when its length ≤ natural length (x ≤ 0). Once slack, T = 0 and the string has no effect. A spring can compress below its natural length — extension becomes negative and the force reverses direction.
Strings can only pull, never push. If your working gives a negative extension for a string, it means the string is slack and T = 0. Springs can do both — check whether the question specifies a string or a spring.
In energy problems, always identify your reference level for GPE at the start. Include ALL forms of energy: kinetic, gravitational PE, and elastic PE. Draw a clear before-and-after diagram.
Further Dynamics Ch 8
F = ma (variable forces)
Newton's second law still applies when forces vary with time or position:
F(t) = ma = m(dv/dt)
F(x) = ma = mv(dv/dx)
Solve by integrating or using calculus techniques from kinematics.
Momentum
Momentum: p = mv
Conservation: m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂
Momentum is conserved when no external forces act on the system. Always define a positive direction first.
Impulse
Impulse = change in momentum
I = mv − mu = Ft  (for constant force)
I = ∫F dt  (for variable force)
Impulse is a vector. The direction matters — a force in the negative direction produces negative impulse.
Collisions
In a collision, momentum is conserved. For the impulse on each object:
Impulse on A = −(Impulse on B)
Newton's third law: equal and opposite impulses.
ExampleA 2 kg ball at 5 m/s hits a 3 kg ball at rest. After: 2 kg ball moves at 1 m/s.
2(5) + 3(0) = 2(1) + 3v → v = 8/3 m/s
Newton's experimental law
For direct collisions, the coefficient of restitution e relates the speeds:
e = (speed of separation) / (speed of approach)
0 ≤ e ≤ 1
e = 1: perfectly elastic (KE conserved). e = 0: perfectly inelastic (objects coalesce).
Always define your positive direction clearly and stick to it. Sign errors in momentum problems are extremely common. Draw a diagram showing velocities before and after the collision.
When using impulse with a variable force, you must integrate F with respect to t. The integral ∫F dt gives the total impulse, which equals the total change in momentum.
Statics Ch 9
Equilibrium conditions
A body is in equilibrium when:
ΣF = 0  (net force is zero)
ΣM = 0  (net moment about any point is zero)
Resolve forces horizontally and vertically, and take moments about a suitable point.
Moments
Moment = Force × perpendicular distance from pivot
M = Fd
Take moments about a point where an unknown force acts — this eliminates that unknown from the equation. Clockwise and anticlockwise moments must balance.
Friction
F ≤ μR

F = frictional force
μ = coefficient of friction
R = normal reaction
Friction acts to oppose motion (or the tendency to move). It is a passive force: it takes whatever value is needed up to the limiting value μR.
Limiting friction
When the body is "on the point of sliding" or "about to move", friction is at its maximum:
F = μR  (limiting equilibrium)
In limiting equilibrium, the body is still stationary but any increase in the applied force would cause motion.
Rigid bodies
For a rigid body (e.g., a ladder, beam, plank), consider:
1. Weight acting at the centre of mass
2. Normal reactions at supports/contacts
3. Friction at rough surfaces
4. Applied forces and their points of action
Ladder problemLadder against a smooth wall, rough ground: resolve horizontally (wall reaction = friction), resolve vertically (ground reaction = weight), take moments about the base to find the wall reaction.
Tilting & sliding
A body on the point of tilting: reaction at the tilting edge, zero reaction at the other support.
A body on the point of sliding: F = μR at the sliding surface.
To determine which happens first, find the critical force for each and compare.
When taking moments, always use the PERPENDICULAR distance from the line of action of the force to the pivot point. If the force is at an angle, resolve it first or use Fd sin θ.
Choose your pivot point wisely. Taking moments about a point where unknown forces meet eliminates them from the equation, giving you a single equation with one unknown.
