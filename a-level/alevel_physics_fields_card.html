The Negative Signs

CRITICAL
Gravitational Potential
  • Gravitational potential is ALWAYS negative
  • Defined as zero at infinity — gets more negative as you approach the mass
  • V = −GM/r — that minus sign is not optional
  • Why? Gravitational force is always attractive — you must do work to move away from a mass
g = −dV/dr   —   field strength is the negative gradient of potential
Electric Potential
  • Positive near a positive charge, negative near a negative charge
  • Can be zero, positive, or negative — unlike gravity
  • Similarly: E = −dV/dr

Field Lines vs Equipotentials

VISUALISE

Field lines: show the direction of force on a positive test charge (electric) or test mass (gravitational).

Equipotentials: lines of constant potential, always perpendicular to field lines.

Closer field lines = stronger field. Closer equipotentials = steeper potential gradient = stronger field.

Radial Field (Point Mass / Charge)
Dashed circles = equipotentials. Arrows = field lines (towards mass).
Uniform Field (Parallel Plates / Near Surface)
Parallel lines = uniform field. Equipotentials are evenly spaced.

Orbits

GRAVITATIONAL

For a circular orbit, gravitational force provides the centripetal force.

1
Set gravitational force = centripetal force: GMm/r² = mv²/r
2
Cancel m and one r: v = √(GM/r) — faster orbits are closer to the mass
3
Substitute v = 2πr/T and rearrange: T² = (4π²/GM)r³ — Kepler's third law
Geostationary Orbit
  • Period T = 24 hours (86400 s)
  • Orbits above the equator
  • Rotates in the same direction as the Earth
  • Appears stationary from the ground — used for communications satellites
  • Altitude ≈ 35,800 km above Earth's surface

Common Mistakes

AVOID THESE
Forgetting the negative sign in gravitational potential
V = −GM/r is always negative. If your answer is positive, you've dropped the sign.
Using E = V/d for radial fields
E = V/d only works for uniform fields (parallel plates). For radial fields use E = kQ/r².
Confusing field strength with potential
Field strength = force per unit charge/mass. Potential = energy per unit charge/mass. Different quantities, different units.
Mixing up capacitor charging & discharging equations
Charging: Q = Q&sub0;(1 − e−t/RC) rises to Q&sub0;. Discharging: Q = Q&sub0;e−t/RC falls from Q&sub0;. Look at the curve shape.
