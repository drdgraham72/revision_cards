Business Objectives 3.1
Profit Maximisation
Profit maximisation: the output level where MC = MR (marginal cost equals marginal revenue). The traditional assumption of firm behaviour in neoclassical economics.
Profit maximised where MC = MR (and MC cuts MR from below)
At this point, the firm cannot increase profit by producing one more or one fewer unit. Applies in all market structures.
MC must cut MR from below for it to be a maximum. If MC cuts MR from above, it is a loss-maximising point — examiners check you know this.
Revenue Maximisation
Revenue maximised where MR = 0
Revenue maximisation: the output level where MR = 0 — total revenue is at its highest. Beyond this point, extra units reduce TR.
Occurs at the midpoint of a linear demand curve. A firm may pursue this to grow market share or drive out competitors. Output is higher and price lower than under profit maximisation.
Sales Maximisation
Sales maximised where AC = AR (normal profit constraint)
The firm sells as many units as possible while still covering all costs (making normal profit). Output is even higher than revenue maximisation.
Evaluation: sales maximisation assumes a normal profit constraint. Without it, the firm would produce even more but make a loss. This objective may be pursued by managers seeking growth or market dominance.
Satisficing
Satisficing: achieving a satisfactory level of profit rather than the maximum. Reflects the divorce of ownership and control (principal-agent problem).
Managers may pursue their own goals (perks, quiet life, empire building) rather than maximising shareholder profit. Shareholders lack perfect information to monitor managers fully.
Key linkSatisficing explains why real-world firms rarely operate at MC=MR — managers satisfice because they have different incentives from owners.
Revenue, Costs & Profit 3.1
Revenue Curves
Total Revenue (TR) = Price × Quantity = AR × Q
Average Revenue (AR) = TR ÷ Q = the demand curve
Marginal Revenue (MR) = change in TR from selling one more unit
In perfect competition: AR = MR (horizontal demand curve — price taker). In imperfect competition: MR falls faster than AR (MR has twice the gradient of a linear demand curve).
AR is always the demand curve. MR lies below AR in imperfect competition because to sell one more unit the firm must lower price on ALL units.
Cost Curves
Total Cost (TC) = Fixed Costs (FC) + Variable Costs (VC)
Average Cost (AC) = TC ÷ Q
Marginal Cost (MC) = change in TC from producing one more unit
Fixed costs: do not vary with output (rent, salaries, insurance). Variable costs: vary directly with output (raw materials, wages for hourly workers). Short run: at least one factor of production is fixed. Long run: all factors are variable. MC always cuts AC at the minimum point of AC (if MC < AC, AC is falling; if MC > AC, AC is rising).
Economies of Scale
Economies of scale: falling long-run average costs as output increases. Diseconomies of scale: rising LRAC as output grows beyond optimal.
Internal EoS: technical (larger machines), managerial (specialist managers), financial (lower interest rates), purchasing (bulk buying), marketing (spread ad costs), risk-bearing (diversification). External EoS: benefits from industry concentration — skilled labour pool, supplier networks, shared infrastructure. Diseconomies: communication problems, coordination difficulties, loss of motivation, bureaucracy.
LRAC curve is U-shaped: falling (EoS) → minimum (MES) → rising (diseconomies)
Minimum Efficient Scale (MES): the lowest level of output at which LRAC is minimised. Determines how many firms the market can support.
Normal vs Supernormal Profit
Normal profit: TR = TC (AC = AR). The minimum return to keep the entrepreneur in the industry — an opportunity cost included in AC.
Supernormal profit: TR > TC (AR > AC). Profit above normal — the shaded area between AR and AC × quantity.
Subnormal (loss): TR < TC (AR < AC). Firm may continue in short run if AR > AVC (covering variable costs).
Evaluation: in the long run, supernormal profit attracts new entrants (in contestable markets), driving profit back to normal. Barriers to entry explain why monopolies sustain supernormal profit.
Government Intervention in Markets 3.4
Competition Policy
Competition policy: government measures to promote competition and prevent abuse of market power. In the UK, enforced by the CMA (Competition and Markets Authority).
Merger control: CMA can block mergers that substantially lessen competition. Cartels: illegal agreements between firms to fix prices or restrict output. Abuse of dominance: firms with >40% market share must not engage in predatory pricing, exclusive dealing, or tying.
Evaluation: competition policy may be difficult to enforce — firms can collude tacitly. Also, some monopoly power may be beneficial (dynamic efficiency, R&D investment). Regulators face information asymmetry.
Regulation & Deregulation
Regulation: rules imposed on firms (e.g. price caps, quality standards, environmental rules). In UK utilities: Ofgem, Ofwat, Ofcom use RPI-X price cap formula.
RPI − X price cap: prices can rise by inflation minus an efficiency factor (X). Incentivises cost cutting.
Deregulation: removing barriers to entry and red tape to increase competition. E.g. bus deregulation, opening postal services to competitors.
Deregulation can lead to worse outcomes if natural monopoly exists — duplicating infrastructure (e.g. rail tracks) is wasteful. The market may not deliver universal service.
Privatisation & Nationalisation
Privatisation: transfer of ownership from public to private sector. E.g. BT, British Gas, rail franchises. Arguments for: profit motive → efficiency, access to capital markets, reduced political interference, shareholder accountability. Arguments against: natural monopoly → private monopoly (may need regulation anyway), short-termism, job losses, cherry-picking profitable routes/services. Nationalisation: transfer from private to public sector. Arguments: control of natural monopolies, universal service, long-term investment, no profit extraction.
Evaluation: the key question is whether ownership matters or whether regulation is sufficient. Many privatised firms still require heavy regulation (water, energy), which undermines the efficiency argument.
Market Structures 3.2
Perfect Competition
Perfect competition: many firms, identical products, perfect information, no barriers to entry/exit, firms are price takers (AR = MR = horizontal demand curve).
Short run: firms can earn supernormal profit (AR > AC) or make losses (AR < AC). Long run: supernormal profit attracts entry → supply increases → price falls → only normal profit survives (AR = AC at minimum AC).
Long-run equilibrium: P = MC = AC (min). Both allocative and productive efficiency achieved.
Perfect competition is a theoretical benchmark — no real market meets all conditions. Use it as a comparison point for evaluating other structures.
Monopolistic Competition
Monopolistic competition: many firms, differentiated products (branding, quality, location), low barriers to entry, each firm has some price-setting power.
Short run: can earn supernormal profit (like a mini-monopoly for its differentiated product). Long run: entry of new firms → demand shifts left → only normal profit (AR tangent to AC but NOT at minimum AC). Key result: productively inefficient (not at min AC) and allocatively inefficient (P > MC) in long run. But consumers benefit from variety and choice.
ExamplesRestaurants, hairdressers, clothing retailers — many firms, differentiated but substitutable products.
Oligopoly
Oligopoly: a market dominated by a few large firms, high barriers to entry, interdependence between firms (each firm considers rivals' reactions).
Concentration ratio: the market share of the top N firms (e.g. 5-firm CR = 60% means top 5 firms hold 60% of market).
Kinked demand curve: if a firm raises price, rivals don't follow (elastic above kink → loses customers). If it cuts price, rivals match (inelastic below kink → little gain). Result: prices are sticky/rigid. Collusion: firms agree to act together. Overt/formal: cartels (illegal in UK). Tacit: price leadership, parallel pricing (harder to prove/prosecute). Non-price competition: branding, advertising, loyalty schemes, quality, innovation — common in oligopoly because price wars are destructive.
Evaluation: oligopolies may be dynamically efficient (supernormal profit funds R&D) but allocatively inefficient (P > MC). Interdependence makes outcomes unpredictable — game theory helps analyse this.
Monopoly
Monopoly: a single firm dominates the market (pure monopoly = 100% share; legal definition in UK = 25%+ market share).
Features: high barriers to entry (legal, economies of scale, branding, patents), price maker, downward-sloping demand curve, can sustain supernormal profit in long run. Barriers to entry: economies of scale, patents, brand loyalty, legal barriers, control of resources, high start-up costs, predatory pricing.
Deadweight loss: monopoly restricts output below competitive level (Qm < Qc) and charges higher price (Pm > Pc). Triangle of welfare loss between MC and demand curve.
Efficiency: allocatively inefficient (P > MC), productively inefficient (not at min AC), but potentially dynamically efficient (Schumpeter: supernormal profit → innovation). Natural monopoly: where LRAC is still falling at the level of market demand — one firm can supply the whole market more cheaply than two or more. E.g. water pipes, rail tracks.
Don't automatically assume monopoly is bad. Evaluate case by case: natural monopolies, dynamic efficiency, cross-subsidisation, and contestability all matter.
Contestable Markets
Contestable market: a market with low barriers to entry and exit, where the threat of potential competition constrains incumbent firms' behaviour (even if few firms exist).
Key condition: no sunk costs (costs irrecoverable on exit). Hit-and-run entry is possible — new firms enter if supernormal profit exists, exit if it disappears. Result: even a monopoly or oligopoly behaves competitively if the market is contestable (prices close to AC, normal profit).
Evaluation: true contestability is rare — most markets have some sunk costs (advertising, specialised equipment). But the degree of contestability matters more than whether it is perfect.
Game Theory & Prisoner's Dilemma
Game theory: analyses strategic decision-making where outcomes depend on the actions of other players (firms).
Prisoner's Dilemma (basic)Two firms choose to collude (high price) or cheat (low price). If both collude → best joint outcome. If one cheats while the other colludes → cheater gains most. If both cheat → worst joint outcome. The dominant strategy is to cheat (Nash equilibrium), even though mutual collusion would be better for both.
Nash equilibrium: an outcome where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing strategy.
In one-shot games, cheating dominates. In repeated games, tit-for-tat and reputation effects can sustain collusion — examiners reward this distinction.
The Labour Market 3.3
Demand for Labour (MRP)
Demand for labour is a derived demand — firms hire workers not for their own sake but for the output they produce.
MRP = MPP × MR (Marginal Revenue Product = Marginal Physical Product × Marginal Revenue)
A profit-maximising firm hires workers up to the point where MRP = wage (MC of labour). The MRP curve is the firm's demand curve for labour. Shifts in labour demand: changes in product demand, productivity, price of capital (substitute/complement), technology.
MRP falls due to diminishing marginal returns (in short run, adding workers to fixed capital eventually yields smaller output gains).
Supply of Labour
Individual supply: backward-bending — at high wages, income effect (prefer leisure) dominates substitution effect (work more). Market supply: upward-sloping — higher wages attract more workers to an occupation/industry. Factors affecting supply: wage rate, non-monetary benefits, qualifications/training required, barriers to entry (e.g. professional exams), geographical/occupational mobility, migration, population size.
Evaluation: wage differentials persist because of differences in skills, training costs, risk, unpleasantness, talent scarcity, and labour market imperfections (e.g. discrimination, imperfect information).
Wage Determination
Competitive labour market: wage = intersection of labour supply and demand (MRP). At equilibrium, no shortage or surplus of workers.
Equilibrium wage: where D(L) = S(L), i.e. MRP = W at market level.
Wages differ between occupations due to: skill level, compensating differentials (danger, unsocial hours), trade unions, monopsony power, discrimination, elasticity of demand/supply.
Trade Unions
Trade union: an organisation of workers that collectively bargains with employers over wages, conditions, and employment rights.
Effect on wages: union bargains wage above equilibrium → higher wage but potentially lower employment (excess supply of labour). Bilateral monopoly: trade union (monopoly seller) vs monopsony employer — wage outcome depends on relative bargaining power.
Evaluation: unions can raise wages AND employment if they counteract monopsony power (pushing wage from below competitive level back toward it). Don't assume unions always cause unemployment.
Minimum Wage
A price floor in the labour market set above the equilibrium wage. Competitive market: minimum wage above equilibrium → excess supply of labour (unemployment). Higher paid workers benefit, but some lose jobs. Monopsony: minimum wage can increase BOTH wages and employment (up to the competitive equilibrium point).
Impact depends on: level set relative to equilibrium, elasticity of labour demand, degree of monopsony power, enforcement. Small increases may have negligible disemployment effects — empirical evidence is mixed.
Monopsony Employer
Monopsony: a single (or dominant) buyer of labour. To hire more workers, it must raise the wage for ALL workers — so MC of labour > AC of labour (the supply curve).
Monopsony hires where MC(L) = MRP, but pays the wage on the supply curve (below MRP). Result: lower wage AND fewer workers than competitive equilibrium.
Monopsony is a key reason why minimum wages and trade unions can improve outcomes — they counteract the monopsony's power to suppress wages.
Discrimination
Wage discrimination: paying different wages for equal productivity based on gender, race, age, etc. Illegal under Equality Act 2010 but persists. Causes: employer prejudice, statistical discrimination (using group averages), occupational segregation (horizontal and vertical), monopsony power exploiting less mobile workers. Gender pay gap: partly explained by occupational choice, part-time work, career breaks — but unexplained residual suggests discrimination.
Evaluation: legislation alone is insufficient — enforcement is difficult and cultural/structural barriers persist. Policies include transparency requirements, quotas, education, and addressing occupational segregation.
