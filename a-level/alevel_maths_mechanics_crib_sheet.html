Edexcel · AS/Year 1 · Mechanics
Kinematics Ch 9–10
SUVAT equations
Five equations for constant acceleration. Each omits one variable:
v = u + at               (no s)
s = ut + ½at²           (no v)
s = vt − ½at²           (no u)
v² = u² + 2as           (no t)
s = ½(u + v)t           (no a)
s = displacement, u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, a = acceleration, t = time.
Which to useIdentify the three known quantities and the one you want. Pick the equation that uses exactly those four variables.
Motion graphs
Displacement–time: gradient = velocity. Straight line = constant velocity. Curve = changing velocity.
Velocity–time: gradient = acceleration. Area under graph = displacement.
Acceleration–time: area under graph = change in velocity.
v = ds/dt   (gradient of s–t graph)
a = dv/dt   (gradient of v–t graph)
s = area under v–t graph
Variable acceleration
When acceleration is not constant, use calculus instead of SUVAT:
v = ds/dt    (differentiate displacement)
a = dv/dt    (differentiate velocity)
s = ∫ v dt   (integrate velocity)
v = ∫ a dt   (integrate acceleration)
Use initial conditions to find constants of integration.
Vertical motion under gravity
Free fall: a = g = 9.8 m/s² (downward). Use SUVAT with a = ±9.8.
Taking upward as positive: a = −9.8 m/s²
Taking downward as positive: a = +9.8 m/s²
At the highest point, v = 0. Time up = time down (if returning to same level).
Projectile motion
Resolve into two independent components:
Horizontal: aₓ = 0, so x = uₓt
where uₓ = u cosθ

Vertical: aᵧ = −g, use SUVAT
where uᵧ = u sinθ
Time of flight links both components. At the highest point, vᵧ = 0 but vₓ unchanged.
Sign convention mistakes: once you choose a positive direction, stick with it throughout. Mixing signs is the single most common error in kinematics problems.
Forces & Newton's Laws Ch 10–11
Newton's 1st law
A body remains at rest or moves with constant velocity unless acted on by a resultant force. Zero resultant force means zero acceleration (equilibrium), not zero motion.
Newton's 2nd law
F = ma
The resultant (net) force equals mass times acceleration. F and a are in the same direction. Units: force in Newtons (N), mass in kg, acceleration in m/s².
Newton's 3rd law
If body A exerts a force on body B, then B exerts an equal and opposite force on A. The forces act on different bodies, are the same type, equal in magnitude, opposite in direction.
Key pointThird-law pairs act on DIFFERENT objects. Weight and normal reaction are NOT a third-law pair — they act on the same object.
Free body diagrams
Show ALL forces acting on a single object: weight (down), normal reaction (perpendicular to surface), tension (along string/rope), friction (opposing motion), applied forces. Label magnitudes and directions.
Common forces
Weight: W = mg  (always downward)
Normal reaction: R ⊥ surface
Tension: T along the string
Friction: F ≤ μR  (opposes motion)
Resolving forces
Break forces into perpendicular components. Apply F = ma in each direction independently:
Horizontal: ΣFₓ = maₓ
Vertical:   ΣFᵧ = maᵧ
When resolving forces: draw a clear diagram first. Choose axes wisely — for inclined planes, resolve parallel and perpendicular to the slope to simplify the equations.
Connected Particles & Inclined Planes Ch 11–12
Connected particles (pulleys)
Two masses connected by a light inextensible string over a smooth pulley:
For each mass, apply F = ma separately.
String is inextensible → same magnitude of acceleration for both.
String is light → tension is the same throughout.
Method1. Draw free body diagram for each particle
2. Write F = ma for each (be consistent with positive direction)
3. Solve simultaneous equations for a and T
Towing problems
Vehicle towing a trailer: apply F = ma to the whole system to find acceleration, then to the trailer alone to find tension in the tow-bar.
Whole system: Driving force − Resistance = (M + m)a
Trailer alone: T − R_trailer = ma
Inclined planes
Resolve forces parallel and perpendicular to the slope:
Parallel to slope: mg sinθ (down the slope)
Perpendicular to slope: mg cosθ (into the slope)

Normal reaction: R = mg cosθ
Friction (if present): F = μR = μmg cosθ
F = ma on a slope
Up the slope (positive up):
Applied force − mg sinθ − μmg cosθ = ma

Down the slope (positive down):
mg sinθ − μmg cosθ = ma
On inclined planes: the weight component down the slope is mg sinθ, NOT mg cosθ. The normal reaction is mg cosθ, NOT mg. Mixing these up is extremely common.
For connected particles: always define positive direction consistently. If one mass goes up, the other goes down — make sure your F = ma equations reflect this.
Moments Ch 12–13
Moment of a force
Moment = Force × perpendicular distance from pivot
Units: Newton-metres (N m). A moment is a turning effect. Clockwise and anticlockwise moments are given opposite signs.
Principle of moments
For equilibrium:
Sum of clockwise moments = Sum of anticlockwise moments
Take moments about any point — choosing a point where an unknown force acts eliminates that force from the equation.
Centre of mass
The point at which the entire weight of a body can be considered to act. For a uniform rod or beam, the centre of mass is at the midpoint. For composite shapes, use moments to find the combined centre of mass.
Equilibrium conditions
For a rigid body to be in static equilibrium:
1. Sum of all forces = 0  (no net force)
2. Sum of all moments about any point = 0
Both conditions must hold simultaneously.
Tilting & toppling
Tilting: a body is on the point of tilting when the reaction at one support becomes zero. Take moments about the other support.

Toppling: occurs when the line of action of the weight falls outside the base of support. The object topples about the edge of the base.
Method for tiltingSet R = 0 at the support about to lift off. Take moments about the remaining support. Solve for the unknown force or distance.
When calculating moments: use the PERPENDICULAR distance from the line of action of the force to the pivot, not the direct distance along the beam. If the force is at an angle, resolve it first.
Choose your pivot wisely. Taking moments about the point where the most unknowns act reduces the number of simultaneous equations you need to solve.
