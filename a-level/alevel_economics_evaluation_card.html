Evaluation — The Difference Between B and A*

How to build analytical depth · not just "however" · chain your reasoning

"However, if demand is price inelastic, as is typical for addictive goods like cigarettes (PED ≈ -0.4), the tax will raise revenue but reduce consumption by only a small proportion. This means the externality persists, and alternative policies such as regulation or information provision may be more effective at reducing the welfare loss."
2. The Five Evaluation Tools
Tool 1
Short Run vs Long Run
"In the short run X, but in the long run Y because..."
Micro: Firms earn supernormal profit in SR, but LR entry erodes it (contestability).
Macro: Fiscal stimulus boosts AD in SR, but may cause inflation in LR near full capacity.
Labour: Minimum wage may cause unemployment in SR, but firms adjust via productivity gains in LR.
Tool 2
Magnitude / Significance
"The impact depends on the size of..."
When a small change makes the theory irrelevant. A 1% tariff vs a 50% tariff. A minimum wage 5p above equilibrium vs £3 above. The multiplier being 1.2 vs 2.5. Always ask: how big is the effect? If the shift is tiny, the theoretical impact may be negligible in practice.
Tool 3
Assumptions
"This assumes... but in reality..."
Ceteris paribus breaking down — the real world doesn't hold other things equal. Rational economic agents not being rational (bounded rationality, habitual behaviour, herd mentality). Perfect information not existing. Factor mobility being limited. Every model has assumptions — challenge them.
Tool 4
Context / It Depends On
"The effectiveness depends on the state of the economy / the elasticity / the market structure..."
A policy that works in a recession may cause inflation at full employment. Fiscal policy is more effective with a high multiplier (high MPC, closed economy). The impact of a tax depends on PED. Monopoly analysis changes if the market is contestable. Context is everything.
Tool 5
Alternative Policies
"An alternative approach would be... which may be more effective because..."
Don't just critique — propose a better solution. If indirect tax doesn't reduce consumption of a demerit good, suggest regulation or information provision. If monetary policy hits the zero lower bound, suggest fiscal stimulus or QE. Examiners love students who can compare policy options and explain trade-offs.
3. Evaluation Chains — 6 Worked Examples
Example 1
Tax on Sugar
Bare Assertion (C grade)
"However, the tax on sugar may not work."
Tax on sugar → PED matters → addictive so inelastic → revenue but not behaviour change → need regulation too
Full Evaluation Chain (A*)
However, the effectiveness of a sugar tax depends critically on the price elasticity of demand. If sugary drinks are habitual or mildly addictive, demand is likely to be price inelastic, meaning the tax will raise significant government revenue but produce only a modest reduction in consumption. The UK Soft Drinks Industry Levy (2018) did reduce sugar content through reformulation, but this was because producers reformulated to avoid the tax — not because consumers switched away. This suggests that regulation (e.g. banning certain sugar levels) or information provision (clearer labelling) may be more effective at reducing the health externality than a tax alone.
Example 2
Interest Rate Cut
Bare Assertion (C grade)
"However, cutting interest rates might not increase spending."
Interest rate cut → depends on confidence → if confidence low, liquidity trap → consumers save not spend → QE as alternative
Full Evaluation Chain (A*)
However, the transmission mechanism from lower interest rates to higher AD depends heavily on consumer and business confidence. If the economy is in a deep recession and expectations are pessimistic, households may choose to save rather than spend despite cheaper borrowing — Keynes described this as a liquidity trap. Post-2008, the Bank of England cut rates to 0.5% yet lending and spending remained subdued, forcing the MPC to adopt quantitative easing (£375bn by 2012) as an alternative. This demonstrates that monetary policy alone may be insufficient when confidence is the binding constraint, and fiscal stimulus may be required alongside it.
Example 3
Minimum Wage
Bare Assertion (C grade)
"However, a minimum wage could cause unemployment."
Minimum wage → depends on level relative to equilibrium → small rise = minimal unemployment → monopsony changes analysis entirely
Full Evaluation Chain (A*)
However, the employment effect of a minimum wage depends on its level relative to the market equilibrium wage and the structure of the labour market. A modest increase above equilibrium may cause only minimal unemployment, as firms absorb the cost through reduced profit margins or higher productivity. Crucially, if the employer is a monopsony (as is common in sectors like care work and retail), the firm is already paying below the competitive wage, and a minimum wage can simultaneously increase both wages and employment — moving the market towards the competitive outcome. Card and Krueger's (1994) study of New Jersey fast-food workers found no significant employment loss from a minimum wage rise, consistent with monopsony power in the labour market.
Example 4
Free Trade
Bare Assertion (C grade)
"However, free trade doesn't always benefit everyone."
Free trade → depends on comparative advantage → but assumes factor mobility → structural unemployment in transition → retraining needed
Full Evaluation Chain (A*)
However, the gains from free trade predicted by comparative advantage theory rest on the assumption of perfect factor mobility — that workers displaced from uncompetitive industries can smoothly transition into growing sectors. In practice, factor mobility is severely limited: a steelworker in Port Talbot cannot easily become a software developer in London. This creates structural unemployment during the transition period, concentrated in specific regions and demographics. The deindustrialisation of the US Rust Belt following NAFTA illustrates this clearly. This means free trade should be accompanied by substantial government investment in retraining, education, and regional development to ensure the gains are distributed more equitably.
Example 5
Monopoly
Bare Assertion (C grade)
"However, monopoly isn't always bad."
Monopoly is bad → but dynamic efficiency → supernormal profit funds R&D → depends on contestability → regulation as alternative
Full Evaluation Chain (A*)
However, the static inefficiency argument against monopoly (P > MC, deadweight loss) overlooks dynamic efficiency. A monopolist earning supernormal profit has both the funds and the incentive to invest in R&D, potentially leading to innovation that benefits consumers in the long run — Schumpeter's "creative destruction" argument. Whether this occurs depends on the contestability of the market: if barriers to entry are high and there is no competitive threat, the monopolist may simply extract rent without innovating (X-inefficiency). This suggests that the optimal policy is not necessarily to break up monopolies, but to ensure contestability through low barriers to entry, or to regulate conduct (e.g. CMA investigations, price caps) while preserving the scale needed for R&D investment.
Example 6
Fiscal Expansion
Bare Assertion (C grade)
"However, fiscal expansion might not work."
Fiscal expansion → multiplier size depends on MPC → open economy = high leakages → crowding out if near capacity → time lags
Full Evaluation Chain (A*)
However, the effectiveness of fiscal expansion depends on the size of the multiplier, which is determined by the marginal propensity to consume and the level of leakages. In a small, open economy like the UK, a significant proportion of any increase in income leaks into imports (MPM), taxation (MPT), and savings (MPS), resulting in a multiplier potentially as low as 1.2–1.3. Furthermore, if the economy is already operating near full capacity, increased government borrowing may push up interest rates, crowding out private sector investment and negating the stimulus. There are also implementation time lags of 12–18 months between policy decision and economic impact. This suggests that fiscal expansion is most effective when there is a large negative output gap and spare capacity in the economy — precisely the conditions of a deep recession.
4. Connective Phrases — The Vocabulary of Evaluation
Basic (B grade) Sophisticated (A* grade)
"However" → "This analysis rests on the assumption that..."
"On the other hand" → "The magnitude of the effect is contingent upon..."
"But" → "A critical limitation of this argument is that..."
"It depends" → "The outcome is sensitive to the prevailing level of..."
"Also" → "A further consideration, often overlooked, is..."
"In conclusion" → "On balance, weighing the short-run costs against the long-run gains..."
5. The Golden Rules
1
Always link evaluation back to the QUESTION — not generic evaluation. If the question asks about inflation, your evaluation must explain why the counter-argument matters for inflation specifically.
2
Don't just list counter-arguments — explain WHY they matter. "PED is relevant" scores nothing. "If PED is inelastic, the tax raises revenue but fails to reduce consumption, meaning the policy objective is not achieved" scores highly.
3
Use real-world examples to support evaluation — dates, figures, countries. "The UK sugar tax (2018)", "Bank of England cut rates to 0.1% in March 2020", "US unemployment fell to 3.5% pre-COVID". Specificity signals mastery.
4
Don't sit on the fence in your conclusion — make a JUDGEMENT. "It depends" is not a conclusion. "On balance, the short-run costs are outweighed by the long-run benefits because..." is a conclusion.
5
The best evaluation considers BOTH sides then explains which is more convincing and WHY. Show the examiner you can weigh evidence, not just list it. The student who judges wins; the student who lists draws.
