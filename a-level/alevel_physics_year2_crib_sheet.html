A-Level Physics — Year 2 Crib Sheet

AQA 7408 · Year 2 · Fields, nuclear & thermal
Further Mechanics Topic 6
Circular motion
ω = 2πf = 2π/T
v = rω
a = v²/r = rω²  (centripetal acceleration, always towards centre)
F = mv²/r = mrω²  (centripetal force)
Centripetal force is not a separate force — it is provided by tension, gravity, friction, etc.
Simple harmonic motion
Definition: acceleration is proportional to displacement and directed towards the equilibrium position.
a = −ω²x  (defining equation of SHM)
x = A cos(ωt)  (displacement, if starting at max)
v = −Aω sin(ωt)
v = ±ω√(A² − x²)
v_max = Aω  (at equilibrium, x = 0)
a_max = Aω²  (at extremes, x = ±A)
T = 2π/ω   |   f = 1/T = ω/2π
SHM systems
Mass-spring: T = 2π√(m/k)
Simple pendulum: T = 2π√(l/g)
Period is independent of amplitude (isochronous) for small oscillations.
Energy in SHM
E_k = ½mω²(A² − x²)
E_p = ½mω²x²
E_total = ½mω²A² = constant
KE is maximum at equilibrium (x = 0); PE is maximum at extremes (x = ±A). Total mechanical energy is constant throughout.
Damping
Light damping: amplitude decreases gradually, period barely changes.
Heavy damping: slow return to equilibrium, no oscillation.
Critical damping: fastest return to equilibrium without oscillating (e.g. car suspension).
Forced oscillations & resonance
Forced oscillation: external periodic driving force applied.
Resonance: when driving frequency = natural frequency → maximum amplitude.
Damping reduces the resonance peak and shifts it slightly below the natural frequency.
ExamplesTacoma Narrows Bridge, tuning forks, microwave ovens, MRI
Common mistake: saying centripetal force is a type of force. It is always provided by another force (gravity, tension, friction, normal contact).
Common mistake: confusing a = −ω²x (SHM defining equation) with the SUVAT equations. SHM acceleration is NOT constant — never use SUVAT.
Exam tip: always check whether the question starts at maximum displacement (use cos) or equilibrium (use sin) for x = A cos(ωt) or x = A sin(ωt).
Gravitational Fields Topic 7
Newton's law of gravitation
F = GMm/r²
G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻². Force is always attractive. r is measured centre-to-centre.
Gravitational field strength
g = F/m = GM/r²
Units: N kg⁻¹ or m s⁻². At the Earth's surface g ≈ 9.81 m s⁻². Field lines point towards the mass (attractive field).
Gravitational potential
V = −GM/r
Units: J kg⁻¹. Always negative (zero at infinity). Work done to move unit mass from infinity to that point.
g = −dV/dr  (field strength = negative potential gradient)
Gravitational potential energy
E_p = −GMm/r
The ΔGPE between two points: ΔE_p = mΔV.
Orbital mechanics
For a circular orbit, gravity provides centripetal force:
GMm/r² = mv²/r  →  v = √(GM/r)
T² = (4π²/GM) r³  (Kepler's third law: T² ∝ r³)
Geostationary orbit: T = 24 h, equatorial plane, same direction as Earth's rotation.
Escape velocity
Minimum speed to escape gravitational field (KE = |GPE|):
½mv² = GMm/r  →  v_esc = √(2GM/r)
Common mistake: forgetting that r is from the CENTRE of the mass, not from the surface. For Earth's surface, r = R_Earth.
Common mistake: dropping the negative sign in V = −GM/r. Gravitational potential is always negative.
Exam tip: the g vs r graph has a 1/r² relationship outside the planet and a linear relationship inside (assuming uniform density).
Thermal Physics Topic 11
Internal energy
Internal energy = sum of random kinetic energies + potential energies of all molecules in a system. Increasing temperature increases KE; changing state changes PE (bonds broken/formed).
First law of thermodynamics
ΔU = Q + W
ΔU = change in internal energy, Q = heat supplied to system, W = work done on system.
Sign convention varies — check whether W is work done ON or BY the system in the question.
Ideal gas laws
pV = nRT  (n = number of moles, R = 8.31 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹)
pV = NkT  (N = number of molecules, k = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J K⁻¹)
Boyle's law: pV = const  (at constant T)
Charles's law: V/T = const  (at constant p)
Pressure law: p/T = const  (at constant V)
T must always be in kelvin: T(K) = T(°C) + 273.
Kinetic theory
pV = ⅓Nm<c²>
½m<c²> = &frac32;kT
<c²> = mean square speed. Root mean square speed: c_rms = √<c²>. Average KE of a molecule is proportional to absolute temperature.
Specific heat capacity
Q = mcΔθ
c = energy needed to raise 1 kg by 1 K. Units: J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹.
Specific latent heat
Q = mL
L = energy needed to change state of 1 kg at constant temperature.
L_f = specific latent heat of fusion (solid ↔ liquid).
L_v = specific latent heat of vaporisation (liquid ↔ gas).
Common mistake: using °C instead of kelvin in pV = nRT. Temperature MUST be in kelvin for gas law calculations.
Exam tip: in specific heat capacity experiments, account for energy losses to surroundings — the calculated value will differ from the accepted value. Discuss this in 6-mark questions.
Electric Fields Topic 8
Coulomb's law
F = kQ₁Q₂/r²   where k = 1/(4πε₀)
ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² F m⁻¹   |   k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N m² C⁻²
Force is attractive for unlike charges, repulsive for like charges. r is centre-to-centre.
Electric field strength
E = F/Q  (definition, units: N C⁻¹ or V m⁻¹)
Uniform field: E = V/d  (between parallel plates)
Radial field: E = kQ/r² = Q/(4πε₀r²)
Field lines point from positive to negative. Closer lines = stronger field.
Electric potential
V = kQ/r = Q/(4πε₀r)
Units: V (volts) = J C⁻¹. Can be positive or negative depending on sign of Q (unlike gravity, which is always negative).
E = −dV/dr  (field strength = negative potential gradient)
Comparison: gravity vs electric
Both are inverse-square-law fields. Key difference: gravity is always attractive (one type of mass); electric force can attract or repel (two types of charge). Gravitational potential is always negative; electric potential can be positive or negative.
Capacitance
C = Q/V  (units: farads, F)
C = ε₀A/d  (parallel plate capacitor)
C = ε₀ε_r A/d  (with dielectric, ε_r = relative permittivity)
Energy stored
E = ½CV² = ½QV = ½Q²/C
Energy = area under Q-V graph (straight line through origin for a capacitor).
Capacitor combinations
Parallel: C_total = C₁ + C₂ + ...  (capacitances add)
Series: 1/C_total = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + ...
Note: opposite to resistors! Parallel capacitors share charge; series capacitors share voltage.
Charging & discharging
Discharging: Q = Q₀e^(−t/RC)
V = V₀e^(−t/RC)   |   I = I₀e^(−t/RC)
Charging: Q = Q₀(1 − e^(−t/RC))
Time constant: τ = RC  (time for Q to fall to 37% of Q₀)
ln(Q) = ln(Q₀) − t/RC  (linearise: plot ln Q vs t, gradient = −1/RC)
Common mistake: confusing capacitor series/parallel rules with resistor rules — they are the opposite way round.
Common mistake: forgetting that during charging, current and voltage across the capacitor change exponentially but in opposite directions.
Exam tip: to find time constant from a graph, read off the time at which the value has fallen to 0.37 (i.e. 1/e) of its initial value.
Magnetic Fields Topic 9
Force on a current-carrying wire
F = BIl sinθ
B = magnetic flux density (T), I = current (A), l = length of wire in field (m), θ = angle between wire and field. Use Fleming's left-hand rule to find direction.
Force on a moving charge
F = BQv sinθ
Q = charge (C), v = velocity (m s⁻¹). Force is always perpendicular to velocity → circular path in uniform B field.
r = mv/(BQ)  (radius of circular motion in a magnetic field)
Magnetic flux
Φ = BA cosθ  (units: weber, Wb = T m²)
θ = angle between field and normal to the area. When field is perpendicular to the surface, θ = 0, Φ = BA.
Flux linkage = NΦ = BAN cosθ  (units: Wb turns)
Faraday's law
ε = −N dΦ/dt  (induced EMF = rate of change of flux linkage)
The magnitude of the induced EMF is proportional to the rate of change of flux linkage through the circuit.
Lenz's law
The induced EMF always acts in a direction to oppose the change producing it. This is the reason for the negative sign in Faraday's law. It is a consequence of conservation of energy.
Transformers
V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p  (turns ratio)
For 100% efficiency: V_p I_p = V_s I_s
Step-up: N_s > N_p (increases voltage, decreases current).
Step-down: N_s < N_p (decreases voltage, increases current).
Only works with AC (changing flux needed for induction). Eddy currents cause energy losses — reduced by laminated core.
Common mistake: applying Fleming's left-hand rule to electromagnetic induction problems. LHR is for motor effect (force on a conductor). Use the right-hand rule or Lenz's law for generator/induction problems.
Exam tip: for Faraday's law calculations, sketch the flux linkage vs time graph — the gradient at any point gives the induced EMF at that instant.
Nuclear Physics Topic 10
Radioactive decay
Decay is random and spontaneous. Cannot be predicted for individual nuclei but can be described statistically for large numbers.
N = N₀e^(−λt)  (number of undecayed nuclei)
A = λN = A₀e^(−λt)  (activity, units: Bq = decays s⁻¹)
λ = decay constant (probability of decay per unit time, s⁻¹).
Half-life
t½ = ln 2 / λ = 0.693 / λ
Time for half the undecayed nuclei to decay (or for activity to halve). After n half-lives: N = N₀ / 2ⁿ.
Finding λ from graphPlot ln N (or ln A) vs t: gradient = −λ
Mass-energy equivalence
E = mc²
ΔE = Δmc²  (energy released = mass defect × c²)
1 u = 1.661 × 10⁻²⁷ kg = 931.5 MeV/c²
Mass defect = mass of individual nucleons − mass of assembled nucleus.
Binding energy
Energy required to completely separate all nucleons in a nucleus. Higher binding energy per nucleon = more stable.
BE per nucleon peaks at Fe-56 (≈ 8.8 MeV per nucleon)
Nuclei lighter than Fe can release energy by fusion; nuclei heavier than Fe can release energy by fission. Both move towards higher BE per nucleon.
Nuclear fission
A heavy nucleus splits into two smaller nuclei (+ neutrons + energy).
e.g. ²³⁵U + n → ¹⁴¹Ba + ⁹²Kr + 3n + energy
Chain reaction: released neutrons cause further fissions. Controlled in reactors using moderator (slows neutrons) and control rods (absorb neutrons).
Nuclear fusion
Two light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus (+ energy).
e.g. ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n + energy
Requires extremely high temperatures (> 10⁷ K) to overcome electrostatic repulsion. Powers stars; difficult to sustain on Earth (plasma confinement).
Inverse square law
I = k/r²  (intensity ∝ 1/r² for γ radiation)
Applies to gamma radiation in a vacuum / air (no absorption). Alpha and beta are absorbed too quickly for the law to apply meaningfully over distance.
Corrected count rate = measured rate − background count rate
Common mistake: forgetting to subtract background radiation from measured count rates before performing calculations.
Common mistake: using N = N₀ / 2ⁿ when t is not an exact multiple of the half-life. Use N = N₀e^(−λt) for general calculations.
Exam tip: be prepared to interpret and sketch the binding energy per nucleon curve. Know where Fe sits and why both fission and fusion release energy.
Remember: in decay equations, always check conservation of mass number (top) AND atomic number (bottom).
