A-Level Economics — Theme 4 Crib Sheet

Edexcel Theme 4 · A Global Perspective
Globalisation 4.1
Causes
Globalisation: the increasing integration and interdependence of world economies through trade, capital flows, migration, and the spread of technology.
Causes: trade liberalisation (reduced tariffs via WTO), improved transport (containerisation, air freight), advances in ICT (internet, instant communication), deregulation of financial markets, growth of TNCs, fall of communism (opened new markets).
Evaluation: globalisation is not inevitable or irreversible — protectionism has resurged (e.g. US-China trade tensions, Brexit). Always consider whether the trend is accelerating or fragmenting.
Consequences
Benefits: lower prices (comparative advantage), greater consumer choice, economies of scale, technology transfer, faster economic growth, job creation in developing economies. Costs: structural unemployment in deindustrialised regions, widening inequality (within and between countries), environmental degradation, cultural homogenisation, tax avoidance by TNCs, over-dependence on global supply chains.
Globalisation creates winners and losers within countries — low-skilled workers in developed nations may lose out while consumers and skilled workers gain. Distribution matters as much as aggregate gains.
Role of TNCs
Transnational corporations (TNCs): firms that operate in more than one country, with headquarters in the home country and subsidiaries abroad.
Benefits to host countries: FDI, job creation, technology/skills transfer, tax revenue, infrastructure development. Costs: profit repatriation, exploitation of weak regulations, environmental damage, crowding out domestic firms, transfer pricing to avoid tax.
Evaluation: the impact of TNCs depends on the bargaining power of the host government. Countries with strong institutions and regulations capture more benefit.
Cultural Impact
Spread of Western consumer culture, language (English as lingua franca), media, and brands. Loss of local traditions, languages, and cultural diversity. Counter-argument: globalisation can also spread cultural exchange both ways (e.g. global popularity of K-pop, Bollywood, sushi). Glocalisation — TNCs adapt products to local tastes.
Trade & the Global Economy 4.1
Comparative Advantage
Comparative advantage: a country has a comparative advantage in producing a good if it can produce it at a lower opportunity cost than another country. The basis for mutually beneficial trade.
Opportunity cost = units of good Y given up ÷ units of good X produced
Calculation methodCountry A produces 10 wheat or 5 cloth. OC of 1 wheat = 5/10 = 0.5 cloth. Country B produces 8 wheat or 8 cloth. OC of 1 wheat = 8/8 = 1 cloth. Country A has CA in wheat (lower OC). Country B has CA in cloth.
Assumptions: no transport costs, constant returns, perfect factor mobility within countries, no externalities. These rarely hold — limits the model's real-world applicability.
Comparative advantage explains WHY countries trade, not that free trade is always optimal. Infant industries, strategic trade policy, and terms of trade effects all qualify the conclusion.
Terms of Trade
Terms of trade index = (export price index ÷ import price index) × 100
Improvement: index rises — can buy more imports per unit of exports. Deterioration: index falls — need to export more to buy the same imports. Causes of change: exchange rate movements, relative inflation rates, commodity price changes, changes in productivity, changes in world demand.
Evaluation: improving terms of trade is not always good — if export prices rise because of lost competitiveness, export volumes fall. The effect on the current account depends on price elasticities.
WTO & Trade Liberalisation
WTO: promotes free trade through multilateral negotiations, dispute resolution, and rules-based trading. Principles: most-favoured nation (equal treatment), reciprocity, transparency. Protectionism: tariffs (tax on imports), quotas (quantity limits), subsidies to domestic producers, embargoes, administrative barriers, regulations.
Tariff diagram: shifts world supply curve up by tariff amount. Domestic production rises, imports fall, consumer surplus falls, government gains tariff revenue, net welfare loss (two deadweight triangles).
Know the tariff diagram inside out — it appears frequently. The two welfare loss triangles represent production inefficiency (domestic firms produce at higher cost than world price) and consumption inefficiency (consumers priced out).
Trading Blocs
Free trade area (FTA): members remove tariffs between themselves but keep individual external tariffs (e.g. USMCA, EFTA). Customs union: FTA + common external tariff (e.g. EU customs union). Single/common market: customs union + free movement of factors (labour, capital). Economic/monetary union: single market + common currency/policy (e.g. eurozone).
Trade creation: bloc membership shifts production to lower-cost member countries — welfare gain. Trade diversion: bloc shifts production from efficient non-members to less efficient members — welfare loss.
Evaluation: whether a trading bloc is welfare-enhancing depends on the balance of trade creation vs trade diversion. Larger blocs with diverse economies tend to create more trade.
Poverty & Inequality 4.2
Absolute vs Relative Poverty
Absolute poverty: income below the level needed to afford basic necessities (food, shelter, clean water). World Bank threshold: $2.15/day (2022 PPP). Relative poverty: income below a percentage of the median income in a specific country (e.g. below 60% of UK median).
A country can reduce absolute poverty while relative poverty rises (if growth is unevenly distributed).
Don't confuse the two — a country like the UK has virtually no absolute poverty but significant relative poverty. Developing countries focus on absolute; developed countries on relative.
Gini Coefficient & Lorenz Curve
Gini coefficient = Area between Lorenz curve and line of equality ÷ Total area under line of equality. Ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality).
Lorenz curve: plots cumulative % of income against cumulative % of population. The further the curve bows from the 45-degree line of equality, the greater the inequality.
Evaluation: the Gini coefficient is a single number and can mask different patterns of inequality (e.g. two countries with the same Gini may have very different distributions). Use alongside other measures.
Causes & Policies
Causes of inequality: wage differentials, unemployment, wealth inheritance, tax system, globalisation (skilled vs unskilled), education access, discrimination, regional disparities. Policies: progressive taxation, transfer payments (benefits), minimum wage, education/training investment, inheritance tax, universal basic services, anti-discrimination legislation.
Evaluation: redistribution faces trade-offs — high taxes may reduce incentives (Laffer curve), generous benefits may create dependency (poverty trap). The equity-efficiency trade-off is central to this topic.
Balance of Payments 4.2
Structure
Balance of payments: a record of all financial transactions between a country and the rest of the world over a period. It must always balance (current account + capital/financial account + errors & omissions = 0).
Current account: trade in goods, trade in services, primary income (investment income, wages), secondary income (transfers, aid). Capital account: capital transfers (e.g. debt forgiveness, migrant transfers). Small in practice. Financial account: FDI, portfolio investment, reserve assets, other investment (bank loans).
Current Account Deficit/Surplus
Current account balance = exports − imports + net primary income + net secondary income
Causes of deficit: strong exchange rate (makes exports expensive, imports cheap), high consumer spending on imports, low productivity/competitiveness, deindustrialisation, high relative inflation. Causes of surplus: weak exchange rate, competitive exports, high savings rate (low import demand), natural resource exports.
A current account deficit is not automatically "bad" — it may reflect strong domestic demand and investment. A persistent structural deficit is more concerning than a cyclical one.
Policies to Correct Deficit
Expenditure-reducing: deflationary fiscal/monetary policy — reduces national income → reduces import spending. But costs: unemployment, lower growth. Expenditure-switching: devaluation/depreciation of exchange rate — makes exports cheaper, imports dearer. But: depends on Marshall-Lerner condition and J-curve effect (see exchange rates). Supply-side: improve competitiveness through education, training, infrastructure, R&D, deregulation. Long-term but addresses root causes.
Evaluation: expenditure-reducing policies work but cause recession. Expenditure-switching depends on elasticities. Supply-side policies are slow. There is no painless solution to a structural deficit.
Exchange Rates 4.2
Exchange Rate Systems
Exchange rate: the price of one currency in terms of another. Determines the cost of imports and the revenue from exports in domestic currency terms.
Floating: determined by market forces of supply and demand for the currency (e.g. GBP, USD). No government intervention. Fixed: government/central bank pegs the rate to another currency and intervenes to maintain it (buying/selling reserves, adjusting interest rates). Managed float: primarily market-determined but central bank intervenes to smooth fluctuations or prevent excessive volatility.
Know the trade-offs: floating gives monetary policy independence but creates uncertainty. Fixed gives stability but requires large reserves and sacrifices independent monetary policy.
Factors Affecting Exchange Rates
Interest rates: higher rates → hot money inflows → demand for currency rises → appreciation. Inflation: higher relative inflation → exports less competitive → demand for currency falls → depreciation. Current account: surplus → demand for currency from exports → appreciation. FDI/capital flows: inward investment → demand for currency → appreciation. Speculation: if markets expect depreciation → sell currency → self-fulfilling prophecy. Quantitative easing: increases money supply → depreciation.
Marshall-Lerner & J-Curve
Marshall-Lerner condition: a depreciation improves the current account ONLY IF |PED(X)| + |PED(M)| > 1 (sum of export and import price elasticities exceeds 1).
J-curve: in the short run after depreciation, the current account worsens (contracts already priced, demand inelastic) before improving in the long run as volumes adjust.
Why the J-curve happensImmediately after depreciation: import prices rise but volumes haven't adjusted (contracts, habits) → import bill rises. Export volumes take time to respond. Over time, demand adjusts → current account improves.
Evaluation: Marshall-Lerner is a long-run condition. In practice, most economies meet it eventually, but the J-curve means policymakers need patience. The short-term worsening can be politically difficult.
Emerging & Developing Economies 4.3
Indicators of Development
Economic growth (rising real GDP) is not the same as economic development (improving living standards, health, education, freedom, and well-being).
HDI = geometric mean of: life expectancy index, education index (mean + expected years of schooling), GNI per capita index (PPP $). Ranges from 0 to 1.
Other indicators: GDP per capita (PPP), literacy rate, infant mortality, access to clean water, Gini coefficient, gender inequality index, multidimensional poverty index.
GDP per capita alone is a poor measure of development — it ignores distribution, non-market activity, environmental costs, and quality of life. Always use multiple indicators.
Barriers to Growth
Primary product dependency: volatile commodity prices, declining terms of trade, Dutch disease. Savings gap: low incomes → low savings → low investment → low growth (Harrod-Domar model).
Harrod-Domar: Growth = savings ratio ÷ capital-output ratio. Low savings → low growth (without external finance).
Other barriers: corruption, weak institutions/governance, debt burden, conflict/political instability, geography (landlocked, disease), brain drain, population growth outstripping economic growth, inadequate infrastructure.
Strategies for Development
Aid: bilateral/multilateral/NGO. Can fill savings gap but risks: dependency, corruption, tied aid, crowding out domestic enterprise. Trade liberalisation: opens markets but infant industries may be destroyed. Need fair trade rules. FDI: brings capital, technology, jobs. Risks: profit repatriation, exploitation, environmental damage. Microfinance: small loans to entrepreneurs (e.g. Grameen Bank). Empowers women, creates local enterprise. But: high interest rates, limited scale, over-indebtedness. Debt relief: e.g. HIPC initiative. Frees government revenue for development spending. But: moral hazard (encourages future borrowing).
Evaluation: no single strategy is sufficient — development requires a combination of aid, trade, investment, good governance, and institutional reform. Context matters: what works in one country may fail in another.
Financial Markets 4.4
Role of Financial Markets
Financial markets channel funds from savers to borrowers, enabling investment, consumption smoothing, and risk management. They facilitate economic growth by allocating capital to its most productive uses.
Functions: facilitate saving, lend to businesses and individuals, facilitate exchange of goods/services (payments), provide forward markets (hedging risk), provide a market for equities (shares — raising capital). Types: money markets (short-term, <1 year — e.g. Treasury bills), capital markets (long-term — e.g. bonds, equities), foreign exchange markets, derivatives markets.
Market Failure in Financial Markets
Asymmetric information: borrowers know more about their risk than lenders → adverse selection (risky borrowers seek loans) and moral hazard (borrowers take excessive risk after getting the loan). Externalities: bank failure creates systemic risk — contagion spreads to other banks and the real economy. Too big to fail: implicit government guarantee → moral hazard (banks take excessive risk knowing they'll be bailed out). Speculation and bubbles: herd behaviour, irrational exuberance → asset price bubbles → crash → recession. E.g. 2007-08 financial crisis: subprime mortgages, securitisation, lack of regulation.
The 2007-08 crisis is the key case study — know the chain: subprime lending → securitisation (CDOs) → credit rating failure → bank insolvency → credit crunch → recession. Examiners expect specific knowledge.
Role of Central Banks
Central bank (Bank of England): sets monetary policy, acts as lender of last resort, regulates financial system, manages exchange rate, issues currency.
Monetary Policy Committee (MPC): sets Bank Rate to meet the 2% CPI inflation target. Transmission mechanism: Bank Rate → market interest rates → borrowing/saving/spending → aggregate demand → inflation. Quantitative easing (QE): central bank creates money to buy government bonds → increases money supply, lowers long-term interest rates, boosts asset prices, stimulates spending. Used when Bank Rate is near zero (lower bound). Lender of last resort: provides emergency liquidity to solvent but illiquid banks — prevents bank runs and systemic collapse. But: creates moral hazard if banks expect to always be rescued.
Evaluation: central bank independence is crucial for credibility — politically motivated monetary policy leads to higher inflation expectations. But independence creates democratic accountability concerns.
Financial Regulation
Macroprudential regulation: systemic stability — e.g. capital requirements (Basel III), stress testing, leverage ratios, countercyclical capital buffers. Microprudential regulation: individual firm stability — e.g. liquidity requirements, conduct regulation, consumer protection. Post-2008 reforms: higher capital ratios, ring-fencing (separating retail and investment banking), Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) and Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA) in UK.
Capital adequacy ratio = bank's capital ÷ risk-weighted assets. Basel III requires minimum 8% (with additional buffers).
Regulation faces trade-offs: too little → financial crises; too much → restricts lending, reduces economic growth, drives activity to unregulated shadow banking sector. Regulators are always fighting the last crisis.
