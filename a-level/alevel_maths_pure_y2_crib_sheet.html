Further Trigonometry Ch 6–7
Reciprocal trig functions
\(\sec\theta = \dfrac{1}{\cos\theta}\)  ·  \(\csc\theta = \dfrac{1}{\sin\theta}\)  ·  \(\cot\theta = \dfrac{1}{\tan\theta} = \dfrac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}\)
\(\sec\) is undefined where \(\cos\theta = 0\), \(\csc\) where \(\sin\theta = 0\), \(\cot\) where \(\tan\theta = 0\). Graphs are reflections/reciprocals of the standard trig curves with vertical asymptotes at the zeros.
Pythagorean identities
\(\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta \equiv 1\)
\(\sec^2\theta \equiv 1 + \tan^2\theta\)   (divide by \(\cos^2\theta\))
\(\csc^2\theta \equiv 1 + \cot^2\theta\)   (divide by \(\sin^2\theta\))
The second and third identities are obtained by dividing the first by \(\cos^{2}\theta\) and \(\sin^{2}\theta\) respectively.
Compound angle formulas
\(\sin(A \pm B) = \sin A\cos B \pm \cos A\sin B\)
\(\cos(A \pm B) = \cos A\cos B \mp \sin A\sin B\)
\(\tan(A \pm B) = \dfrac{\tan A \pm \tan B}{1 \mp \tan A\tan B}\)
Double angle formulas
\(\sin 2A = 2\sin A\cos A\)
\(\cos 2A = \cos^2 A - \sin^2 A = 2\cos^2 A - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2 A\)
\(\tan 2A = \dfrac{2\tan A}{1 - \tan^2 A}\)
The \(\cos 2A\) forms rearrange to give half-angle substitutions for integration:
\(\cos^2 A = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2A)\)
\(\sin^2 A = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 - \cos 2A)\)
R-formula (harmonic form)
\(a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta = R\sin(\theta + \alpha)\)
\(a\cos\theta + b\sin\theta = R\cos(\theta - \alpha)\)
where \(R = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}\), \(\tan\alpha = \dfrac{b}{a}\)
Used to solve equations of the form \(a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta = c\), and to find max/min values of trig expressions.
Example\(3\sin\theta + 4\cos\theta = R\sin(\theta+\alpha)\) → \(R = \sqrt{9+16} = 5\), \(\tan\alpha = \frac{4}{3}\) → \(\alpha \approx 53.1°\)
Small angle approximations
\(\sin\theta \approx \theta\)   ·   \(\cos\theta \approx 1 - \dfrac{\theta^2}{2}\)   ·   \(\tan\theta \approx \theta\)
Valid when \(\theta\) is small and measured in radians. Used for limits and approximations.
\(\cos(A \pm B)\): the sign FLIPS — \(\cos(A+B)\) uses minus, \(\cos(A-B)\) uses plus. The opposite of what you might expect.
For R-formula questions: always state \(R\) and \(\alpha\), then rewrite the expression before solving. Max value of \(R\sin(\theta+\alpha)\) is \(R\), occurring when \(\sin(\theta+\alpha) = 1\).
Further Differentiation Ch 9–10
Chain rule
\(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy}{du} \times \dfrac{du}{dx}\)
For composite functions \(y = f(g(x))\): differentiate the outer function, multiply by the derivative of the inner.
Example\(y = (3x+1)^{5}\) → \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 5(3x+1)^{4} \times 3 = 15(3x+1)^{4}\)
Product rule
\(\dfrac{d(uv)}{dx} = u\dfrac{dv}{dx} + v\dfrac{du}{dx}\)
Example\(y = x^{2}\sin x\) → \(\frac{dy}{dx} = x^{2}\cos x + 2x\sin x\)
Quotient rule
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(\dfrac{u}{v}\right) = \dfrac{v\frac{du}{dx} - u\frac{dv}{dx}}{v^2}\)
Remember: "low d-high minus high d-low, over the square of what's below."
Trig derivatives
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}(\sin x) = \cos x\)
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}(\cos x) = -\sin x\)
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}(\tan x) = \sec^2 x\)
Combined with chain rule: \(\frac{d}{dx}(\sin(kx)) = k\cos(kx)\), etc.
Exponential & log
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}(e^x) = e^x\)  ·  \(\dfrac{d}{dx}(e^{kx}) = ke^{kx}\)
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}(\ln x) = \dfrac{1}{x}\)  ·  \(\dfrac{d}{dx}(\ln f(x)) = \dfrac{f'(x)}{f(x)}\)
Implicit differentiation
Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\). When differentiating a \(y\)-term, multiply by \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) (chain rule).
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}(y^2) = 2y \cdot \dfrac{dy}{dx}\)
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}(xy) = x\dfrac{dy}{dx} + y\)  (product rule)
Then rearrange to isolate \(\frac{dy}{dx}\).
Parametric differentiation
\(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}\)
For curves defined as \(x = f(t)\), \(y = g(t)\). Differentiate each with respect to \(t\), then divide.
Connected rates
\(\dfrac{dA}{dt} = \dfrac{dA}{dr} \times \dfrac{dr}{dt}\)
Chain rule applied to real-world rates. Identify which rate you know, which you want, and find the connecting variable.
Quotient rule: the order matters — it is \(v\frac{du}{dx}\) MINUS \(u\frac{dv}{dx}\), not the other way round. Getting this backwards is the most common mistake.
For implicit differentiation: collect all \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) terms on one side, factor out \(\frac{dy}{dx}\), then divide. Treat \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) as a single object you are solving for.
Differential Equations Ch 11
Separable variables
If \(\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x)g(y)\), separate and integrate:
\(\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{g(y)}\, dy = \int f(x)\, dx\)
Example\(\frac{dy}{dx} = xy\) → \(\int\frac{1}{y}\,dy = \int x\,dx\) → \(\ln|y| = \frac{x^{2}}{2} + c\) → \(y = Ae^{x^{2}/2}\)
Particular solutions
Use boundary/initial conditions to find the constant of integration. Substitute the given point \((x_{0}, y_{0})\) into the general solution and solve for \(c\) (or \(A\)).
Modelling
\(\dfrac{dP}{dt} = kP \;\to\; P = P_0 e^{kt}\)  (exponential growth/decay)
\(\dfrac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_0)\) → Newton's cooling
Set up the differential equation from the word problem, solve by separation, then apply conditions.
When separating variables, do not forget to integrate BOTH sides. And always include \(+c\) on one side (not both).
After solving, always substitute back to check your solution satisfies the original DE and the boundary conditions.
Further Integration Ch 11
Standard results
\(\int e^x\, dx = e^x + c\)  ·  \(\int e^{kx}\, dx = \tfrac{1}{k}e^{kx} + c\)
\(\int \tfrac{1}{x}\, dx = \ln|x| + c\)
\(\int \sin x\, dx = -\cos x + c\)  ·  \(\int \cos x\, dx = \sin x + c\)
\(\int \sec^2 x\, dx = \tan x + c\)
Integration by substitution
Replace a complicated part with \(u\), find \(\frac{du}{dx}\), substitute everything including \(dx\):
\(\int f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\, dx\) → let \(u = g(x)\), \(du = g'(x)\,dx\) → \(\int f(u)\, du\)
For definite integrals: change the limits to \(u\)-values, or convert back to \(x\) at the end.
Example\(\int 2x(x^{2}+1)^{3}\,dx\), let \(u = x^{2}+1\), \(du = 2x\,dx\) → \(\int u^{3}\,du = \frac{u^{4}}{4} + c = \frac{(x^{2}+1)^{4}}{4} + c\)
Integration by parts
\(\displaystyle\int u\, dv = uv - \int v\, du\)
Choose \(u\) using LIATE priority: Logs, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exponential.
Example\(\int x e^{x}\,dx\): \(u = x\), \(dv = e^{x}\,dx\) → \(du = dx\), \(v = e^{x}\) → \(xe^{x} - \int e^{x}\,dx = xe^{x} - e^{x} + c\)
Sometimes need to apply twice (e.g. \(\int x^{2}e^{x}\,dx\)) or use the "cycling" trick for \(\int e^{x}\sin x\,dx\).
Partial fractions
Split rational functions before integrating:
\(\dfrac{A}{x-a}\) integrates to \(A\ln|x-a| + c\)
\(\dfrac{A}{(x-a)^2}\) integrates to \(\dfrac{-A}{x-a} + c\)
Example\(\int \frac{3x+5}{(x+1)(x+2)}\,dx\) → \(\int \left[\frac{2}{x+1} + \frac{1}{x+2}\right]dx = 2\ln|x+1| + \ln|x+2| + c\)
Trapezium rule
\(\displaystyle\int_a^b y\, dx \approx \frac{h}{2}\big[y_0 + 2(y_1+y_2+\cdots+y_{n-1}) + y_n\big]\)
where \(h = \dfrac{b-a}{n}\)
More strips (larger \(n\)) gives a better approximation. Overestimates for convex curves, underestimates for concave.
Parametric integration
\(\displaystyle\int y\, dx = \int y \cdot \frac{dx}{dt}\, dt\)
Substitute \(x = f(t)\), \(y = g(t)\), and change limits to \(t\)-values. The \(dx\) becomes \(\frac{dx}{dt}\,dt\).
By parts: choosing the wrong \(u\) and \(dv\) makes the integral harder, not easier. If it gets more complicated, swap your choice.
For \(\int \sin^{2}x\,dx\) or \(\int \cos^{2}x\,dx\), use the double angle identity: \(\sin^{2}x = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 - \cos 2x)\), \(\cos^{2}x = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + \cos 2x)\).
Sequences & Series Ch 3–4
Arithmetic sequences
\(u_n = a + (n-1)d\)
\(S_n = \dfrac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d) = \dfrac{n}{2}(a + l)\)
\(a\) = first term, \(d\) = common difference, \(l\) = last term. Sum formula: average of first and last, times \(n\).
Geometric sequences
\(u_n = ar^{n-1}\)
\(S_n = \dfrac{a(1 - r^n)}{1 - r} \quad (r \neq 1)\)
\(a\) = first term, \(r\) = common ratio. Find \(r\) by dividing consecutive terms: \(r = u_{2}/u_{1}\).
Sum to infinity
\(S_\infty = \dfrac{a}{1 - r} \quad \text{when } |r| < 1\)
Converges only when \(|r| < 1\). If \(|r| \geq 1\), the series diverges and has no finite sum.
Binomial expansion (rational n)
\((1+x)^n = 1 + nx + \dfrac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \dfrac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3 + \cdots\)
Valid when \(|x| < 1\)
For \((a+bx)^{n}\): factor out \(a^{n}\) first → \(a^{n}(1 + bx/a)^{n}\), then expand with \(|bx/a| < 1\).
Example\((1+x)^{-1} = 1 - x + x^{2} - x^{3} + \cdots\)   (\(|x| < 1\))
Sigma notation
\(\displaystyle\sum_{r=1}^{n} f(r) = f(1) + f(2) + \cdots + f(n)\)
Useful sums: \(\sum r = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}\), \(\sum r^{2} = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}\).
Binomial expansion for rational \(n\) is an INFINITE series, not finite. You must state the validity condition \(|x| < 1\) (or equivalent) — marks are lost without it.
For geometric series: always check \(|r| < 1\) before using \(S_{\infty}\). In proof questions, show that \(r^{n} \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\).
Numerical Methods Ch 8
Sign change method
If f(a) and f(b) have opposite signs AND f is continuous on [a,b], then there is a root in (a,b).
\(f(a) > 0\) and \(f(b) < 0\) (or vice versa) → root exists in \((a, b)\)
Iteration
Rearrange \(f(x) = 0\) into \(x = g(x)\), then iterate:
\(x_{n+1} = g(x_n)\)
Converges when \(|g'(x)| < 1\) near the root. Different rearrangements may converge to different roots or diverge.
Example\(x^{3} = 5x + 1\) → \(x = (5x+1)^{1/3}\), start \(x_{0} = 2\): \(x_{1} = \sqrt[3]{11} \approx 2.224\ldots\)
Newton-Raphson
\(x_{n+1} = x_n - \dfrac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}\)
Usually converges much faster than simple iteration. Can fail if \(f'(x_{n}) \approx 0\) or if the starting point is poor (near a turning point).
Trapezium rule
\(\displaystyle\int_a^b y\, dx \approx \frac{h}{2}\big[y_0 + 2(y_1+\cdots+y_{n-1}) + y_n\big]\)
\(h = \dfrac{b-a}{n}\)
Increasing \(n\) (more strips) improves accuracy. State whether the estimate is an overestimate or underestimate by considering the curve's concavity.
Newton-Raphson can diverge or oscillate if the starting value is near a stationary point — always check that \(f'(x_{n})\) is not close to zero.
In exams, show each iteration step and give values to the required decimal places. State convergence by showing two consecutive iterates agree to the required accuracy.
Vectors (3D) Ch 12
3D vectors
Position vectors in 3D: \(\mathbf{r} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}\) or as column vectors.
\(|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}\)
Unit vector: \(\hat{\mathbf{a}} = \mathbf{a} / |\mathbf{a}|\). Direction ratios give the relative components.
Scalar (dot) product
\(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + a_3b_3 = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta\)
If \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0\), the vectors are perpendicular. Rearrange to find the angle:
\(\cos\theta = \dfrac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|}\)
Angle between vectors
Use the scalar product formula. Always give the acute angle unless told otherwise — if \(\cos\theta\) is negative, the acute angle is \(180° - \theta\).
Vector equation of a line
\(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + \lambda\mathbf{d}\)
\(\mathbf{a}\) = position vector of a known point on the line, \(\mathbf{d}\) = direction vector, \(\lambda\) = parameter.
ExampleLine through \((1,2,3)\) in direction \((4,-1,2)\): \(\mathbf{r} = (1,2,3) + \lambda(4,-1,2)\)
To find if a point lies on the line: substitute and check all three components give the same \(\lambda\).
When finding angles between lines, use the direction vectors — not position vectors. Take \(|\cos\theta|\) if you need the acute angle.
To find the intersection of two lines in 3D, set them equal and solve the simultaneous equations. Check the third equation for consistency — in 3D, lines may be skew (non-intersecting and non-parallel).
