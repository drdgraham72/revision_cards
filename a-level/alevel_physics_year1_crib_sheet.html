Measurements & Errors Topic 1
SI base units
metre (m), kilogram (kg), second (s), ampere (A), kelvin (K), mole (mol), candela (cd)
All derived units reduce to combinations of these seven
SI prefixes
p = 10⁻¹², n = 10⁻⁹, μ = 10⁻⁶, m = 10⁻³, c = 10⁻², k = 10³, M = 10⁶, G = 10⁹, T = 10¹²
pico, nano, micro, milli, centi, kilo, mega, giga, tera
Random vs systematic errors
Random: unpredictable scatter around true value; reduced by repeating & averaging.
Systematic: consistent offset in one direction (e.g. zero error); NOT reduced by repeating — must recalibrate or correct.
Precision vs accuracy
Precise: repeated readings are close together (small spread).
Accurate: readings are close to the true value.
You can be precise but inaccurate (systematic error) or accurate but imprecise (large random error with mean near true value).
Percentage uncertainty
% uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty / measured value) × 100
ExampleLength = 25.0 ± 0.5 cm → % unc = (0.5/25.0) × 100 = 2.0%
Combining uncertainties
Adding/subtracting quantities: ADD absolute uncertainties
Multiplying/dividing quantities: ADD percentage uncertainties
Raising to a power n: MULTIPLY percentage uncertainty by n
Significant figures
Final answer should match the least number of s.f. used in the calculation. Leading zeros don't count. Trailing zeros after a decimal point do count.
Common mistake: giving a final answer to more s.f. than the raw data — this implies false precision.
Exam tip: always state the uncertainty rule you're using when combining uncertainties. Marks are awarded for method.
Particles & Radiation Topic 2
The Standard Model
Quarks (6 flavours, each with antiquark):
up (+⅔e), down (−⅓e), strange (−⅓e), charm (+⅔e), top (+⅔e), bottom (−⅓e)
Leptons (3 generations):
electron (e⁻), muon (μ⁻), tau (τ⁻) + corresponding neutrinos (νₑ, νμ, ντ)
Exchange bosons: photon (γ, EM), W± & Z⁰ (weak), gluon (strong), Higgs (mass)
Hadrons
Particles made of quarks, feel the strong force.
Baryons = 3 quarks (e.g. proton = uud, neutron = udd)
Mesons = quark + antiquark (e.g. pion π⁺ = ud̄)
Antibaryons = 3 antiquarks.
Conservation laws
In every interaction, the following are conserved:
Charge (Q), Baryon number (B), Lepton number (L) — always conserved
Strangeness (S) — conserved in strong & EM interactions, can change by ±1 in weak
Baryon numbersQuarks: B = +⅓ each → baryon B = +1, antibaryon B = −1, meson B = 0
Photon energy
E = hf = hc/λ
h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s, c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹
1 eV = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
Photoelectric effect
hf = φ + E_k(max)
φ = work function (minimum energy to release electron).
Threshold frequency f₀: hf₀ = φ → f₀ = φ/h. Below f₀, no electrons emitted regardless of intensity.
E_k(max) = hf − φ = eV_s (stopping potential)
Wave-particle duality
de Broglie wavelength: λ = h / mv = h / p
All matter has wave properties. Electrons diffract through crystal lattices, confirming wave behaviour.
Common mistake: thinking increasing light intensity below threshold frequency will eventually eject electrons. It won't — each photon must individually have E ≥ φ.
Exam tip: always convert eV to joules (or vice versa) before substituting into E = hf. State the conversion factor.
Waves Topic 3
Progressive waves
Transverse: oscillation perpendicular to direction of travel (e.g. EM waves, water waves).
Longitudinal: oscillation parallel to direction of travel (e.g. sound waves) — compressions & rarefactions.
v = fλ   |   T = 1/f   |   v = λ/T
Wave properties
Amplitude (A): maximum displacement from equilibrium.
Wavelength (λ): distance for one complete cycle.
Frequency (f): number of complete cycles per second (Hz).
Phase difference: measured in degrees or radians. In phase = 0°/360°; antiphase = 180°.
Superposition
When two waves meet, resultant displacement = sum of individual displacements.
Constructive: path difference = nλ (in phase, amplitudes add)
Destructive: path difference = (n+½)λ (antiphase, amplitudes cancel)
Standing waves
Formed by superposition of two progressive waves of same f, λ, amplitude travelling in opposite directions.
Nodes: zero displacement (destructive). Antinodes: maximum displacement (constructive).
1st harmonic: L = λ/2  |  2nd: L = λ  |  3rd: L = 3λ/2
f_n = n × f₁  (nth harmonic)
Young's double slit
λ = ax / D
a = slit separation, x = fringe spacing, D = slit-to-screen distance.
Proves wave nature of light. Requires coherent, monochromatic source.
Diffraction gratings
d sinθ = nλ
d = slit spacing (1/N where N = lines per metre), n = order number.
Sharper maxima than double slit; used to measure λ precisely.
Refraction
Snell's law: n₁ sinθ₁ = n₂ sinθ₂
n = c / v  (refractive index)
Total internal reflection: occurs when θ > critical angle θ_c and light goes from denser to less dense medium.
sin θ_c = n₂ / n₁  (where n₁ > n₂)
Common mistake: using d sinθ = nλ but forgetting d = 1/N (lines per metre, not lines per mm).
Exam tip: for standing waves, sketch the pattern and label nodes/antinodes. Count the number of half-wavelengths that fit in the string length.
Mechanics Topic 4
SUVAT equations
v = u + at
s = ut + ½at²
s = vt − ½at²
v² = u² + 2as
s = ½(u + v)t
s = displacement, u = initial velocity, v = final velocity, a = acceleration, t = time. Only valid for constant acceleration.
Projectile motion
Resolve into two independent components:
Horizontal: constant velocity (a = 0), s_x = v_x × t
Vertical: constant acceleration (a = g = 9.81 m s²), use SUVAT
Air resistance ignored unless stated. Time of flight is the link between components.
Newton's laws
1st: An object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted on by a resultant force (inertia).
2nd: Resultant force = rate of change of momentum.
F = ma  (when mass is constant)
3rd: If A exerts a force on B, B exerts an equal and opposite force on A. Forces act on different objects, same type.
W = mg  (weight, where g = 9.81 m s⁻²)
Moments
Moment = Force × perpendicular distance from pivot
Principle of moments: Σ clockwise moments = Σ anticlockwise moments (in equilibrium)
A couple = two equal, antiparallel forces → torque = F × d (no net force, pure rotation).
Work, energy & power
Work done: W = Fs cosθ
KE = ½mv²   |   GPE = mgh
Power: P = W/t = Fv
Efficiency = useful output energy (or power) / total input energy (or power)
Conservation of energy: total energy in a closed system is constant. Energy transforms between KE, GPE, elastic PE, thermal, etc.
Momentum & impulse
p = mv  (momentum, kg m s⁻¹)
Conservation: total momentum before = total momentum after (in a closed system)
Impulse = FΔt = Δp = mv − mu
Elastic collision: KE conserved. Inelastic: KE not conserved (some → heat/sound). Perfectly inelastic: objects stick together.
Common mistake: forgetting that momentum is a vector — assign positive/negative directions and be consistent.
Common mistake: using SUVAT when acceleration is not constant. Check the question context.
Exam tip: for projectile problems, always separate horizontal and vertical. Write two columns of SUVAT values.
Remember: Newton's 3rd law pairs must be the same TYPE of force (both gravitational, both contact, etc.) acting on DIFFERENT objects.
Materials AQA 3.4
Density
ρ = m / V
ρ = density (kg m⁻³), m = mass (kg), V = volume (m³). Measure regular solids with a ruler; irregular solids by displacement of water.
Hooke's law
F = kΔL
k = spring constant (N m⁻¹), ΔL = extension (m). Linear relationship only up to the limit of proportionality. Beyond this, the graph curves but the spring may still return to original length (elastic limit).
Stress
σ = F / A
σ = stress (Pa or N m⁻²), F = force (N), A = cross-sectional area (m²). Stress is force per unit area — allows comparison between materials of different thickness.
Strain
ε = ΔL / L
ε = strain (dimensionless, no units), ΔL = extension (m), L = original length (m). Strain is the fractional change in length.
Young's modulus
E = σ / ε = (F/A) / (ΔL/L) = FL / AΔL
Units: Pa (same as stress). Gradient of the linear region of a stress-strain graph. A measure of stiffness — higher E means harder to deform.
Typical valuesSteel ≈ 200 GPa, Copper ≈ 130 GPa, Rubber ≈ 0.01 GPa
Elastic vs plastic deformation
Elastic deformation: material returns to original shape when the load is removed. Energy is stored and fully recoverable. Occurs below the elastic limit.
Plastic deformation: material is permanently deformed when the load is removed. Energy is dissipated (not recoverable). Occurs beyond the elastic limit.
Elastic strain energy
E = ½FΔL = ½k(ΔL)²
Energy stored = area under the force-extension graph. For a linear (Hookean) region, the graph is a straight line and the area is a triangle.
Stress-strain graphs
Linear elastic region: straight line from origin, obeys Hooke's law. Gradient = Young's modulus.
Limit of proportionality: end of the linear region.
Elastic limit: beyond this point, permanent deformation begins.
Yield point: stress at which large plastic deformation occurs for little increase in stress.
Ultimate tensile strength (UTS): maximum stress the material can withstand.
Fracture point: where the material breaks.
Material types
Brittle: fractures with little or no plastic deformation (e.g. glass, ceramics). Stress-strain graph is a straight line up to fracture.
Ductile: can undergo significant plastic deformation before fracture (e.g. copper, steel). Shows a clear yield point and necking.
Polymeric: can show very large strains and complex loading/unloading behaviour (e.g. rubber, polythene). Rubber returns to original shape (elastic) but with a hysteresis loop.
Breaking stress
Breaking stress = Force at fracture / original cross-sectional area
Also called ultimate tensile strength (UTS). This is the maximum point on the stress-strain curve before necking and fracture.
Common mistake: confusing the limit of proportionality with the elastic limit. The limit of proportionality is where the graph stops being linear; the elastic limit is where permanent deformation begins. They are close together but not the same point.
Common mistake: forgetting that strain has no units. Writing "Pa" or "N m⁻¹" for strain will lose marks.
Exam tip: when calculating Young's modulus from a graph, use the gradient of the STRESS-STRAIN graph (not force-extension). If given force-extension, convert using the cross-sectional area and original length.
Electricity Topic 5
Current & charge
I = ΔQ / Δt
Current = rate of flow of charge. Conventional current: positive to negative. Electron flow: opposite direction. 1 A = 1 C s⁻¹.
Potential difference
V = W / Q
p.d. = energy transferred per unit charge. 1 V = 1 J C⁻¹.
Resistance
V = IR  (Ohm's law)
Resistivity: R = ρL / A
ρ = resistivity (Ω m), L = length, A = cross-sectional area. Ohm's law only applies to ohmic conductors (constant R at constant temperature).
Power
P = IV = I²R = V²/R
E = Pt = IVt  (energy transferred)
Series circuits
Same current through all components
V_total = V₁ + V₂ + ... (voltages add)
R_total = R₁ + R₂ + ... (resistances add)
Parallel circuits
Same p.d. across all branches
I_total = I₁ + I₂ + ... (currents add)
1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ...
EMF & internal resistance
ε = I(R + r)
V = ε − Ir  (terminal p.d.)
ε = EMF (total energy per unit charge from source), r = internal resistance. "Lost volts" = Ir.
Finding rPlot V vs I: gradient = −r, y-intercept = ε
Kirchhoff's laws
1st law (junction): ΣI_in = ΣI_out (conservation of charge)
2nd law (loop): Σε = ΣIR (conservation of energy around any closed loop)
Potential dividers
V_out = V_in × R₂ / (R₁ + R₂)
Used with LDRs (resistance decreases with light) and thermistors (resistance decreases with temperature) to create sensor circuits.
I-V characteristics
Ohmic conductor: straight line through origin (constant R).
Filament lamp: curve — R increases as temperature rises (more lattice vibrations).
Diode: conducts in forward bias above ~0.6 V; virtually zero current in reverse bias.
Common mistake: using V = IR for the whole circuit when there is internal resistance. Use ε = I(R + r) instead.
Common mistake: forgetting that for parallel resistors, total R is always LESS than the smallest individual R.
Exam tip: sketch I-V graphs from memory — they come up frequently. Label axes (I vertical, V horizontal by convention in physics).
For potential divider questions, check whether the component is R₁ (top) or R₂ (bottom) before applying the formula.
