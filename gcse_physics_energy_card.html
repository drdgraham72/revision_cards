The 8 Energy Stores

🏃
Kinetic
Energy an object has because it is moving
A car driving, a runner sprinting
⛰
Gravitational Potential
Energy stored by an object raised above the ground
A book on a shelf, a skier at the top of a slope
🔫
Elastic Potential
Energy stored when an object is stretched or compressed
A stretched rubber band, a compressed spring
🔥
Thermal
Energy stored in an object because of its temperature
A hot cup of tea, a warm radiator
⚡
Chemical
Energy stored in chemical bonds — released by reactions
Food, batteries, fuel
🧲
Magnetic
Energy stored by magnets attracting or repelling
Two magnets pulling together, a fridge magnet
⚡
Electrostatic
Energy stored by electric charges attracting or repelling
A charged balloon sticking to a wall, lightning
☢
Nuclear
Energy stored in the nucleus of an atom
Nuclear power stations, the Sun

Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed —
only transferred between stores.
The total energy before a transfer is always equal to the total energy after. Energy is never "used up" — it just moves from one store to another. Some always ends up in thermal stores (heat) and is dissipated to the surroundings.
Common exam phrasing: "Describe the energy transfers..."

How to answer: Name the starting energy store, say how it is transferred (mechanically / electrically / by heating / by radiation), and name the final energy store(s). Always mention wasted energy going to the thermal store of the surroundings.

Example answer: "Energy is transferred from the chemical store of the fuel to the kinetic store of the car (mechanically), with some energy dissipated to the thermal store of the surroundings by heating."

Specific Heat Capacity

\( E = mc\Delta T \)
E
Energy transferred
Joules (J)
m
Mass
Kilograms (kg)
c
Specific heat capacity
J/kg°C
ΔT
Temperature change
°C
Find E
\( E = mc\Delta T \)
Find \( m \)
\( m = \frac{E}{c\Delta T} \)
Find \( \Delta T \)
\( \Delta T = \frac{E}{mc} \)
How much energy is needed to heat 2 kg of water by 50°C? (c = 4200 J/kg°C)
\( E = m \times c \times \Delta T \)
\( E = 2 \times 4200 \times 50 \)
\( E = 420{,}000 \text{ J} \)
E = 420,000 J (or 420 kJ)
Required Practical: Investigating Specific Heat Capacity
  1. Measure the mass of the material block (in kg)
  2. Insert a thermometer and an electric heater into the block
  3. Record the starting temperature
  4. Turn on the heater and use a joulemeter to measure energy supplied
  5. Record the temperature every minute for a set time
  6. Use \( E = mc\Delta T \), rearranged to \( c = \frac{E}{m\Delta T} \)
Common mistake: Forgetting to convert grams to kilograms! Mass MUST be in kg. If you are given 500g, that is 0.5 kg.
6

Power

\( P = \dfrac{E}{t} \)
Power = Energy transferred ÷ time
\( P = \dfrac{W}{t} \)
Power = Work done ÷ time
P = Power (Watts, W)
E = Energy (Joules, J)
W = Work done (Joules, J)
t = Time (seconds, s)
1 kilowatt (kW) = 1000 watts (W)
A kettle transfers 360,000 J of energy in 3 minutes. What is its power?
Convert time: 3 minutes = 180 seconds
\( P = \frac{E}{t} \)
\( P = \frac{360{,}000}{180} \)
\( P = 2000 \text{ W} \)
P = 2000 W (or 2 kW)

Kinetic Energy & GPE

Kinetic Energy
\( KE = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \)
KE = kinetic energy (J)
m = mass (kg)
v = speed (m/s)
\( v \) is SQUARED! Doubling speed = \( 4\times \) the KE!
Tripling speed = \( 9\times \) the KE!
Gravitational Potential Energy
\( GPE = mgh \)
GPE = gravitational PE (J)
m = mass (kg)
g = gravitational field strength (N/kg)
h = height (m)

g = 10 N/kg on Earth (given in exam)
Falling object: GPE transfers to KE
GPE (max)
→
falling
KE (max)
+
thermal (air resistance)
At the top: all GPE, no KE. At the bottom: all KE, no GPE.
A 1200 kg car is travelling at 15 m/s. Calculate its kinetic energy.
\( KE = \tfrac{1}{2} \times m \times v^2 \)
\( KE = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 1200 \times 15^2 \)
\( KE = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 1200 \times 225 \)
\( KE = 135{,}000 \text{ J} \)
KE = 135,000 J (or 135 kJ)
A 0.5 kg ball is thrown to a height of 12 m. Calculate the GPE gained. (g = 10 N/kg)
\( GPE = m \times g \times h \)
\( GPE = 0.5 \times 10 \times 12 \)
\( GPE = 60 \text{ J} \)
GPE = 60 J
