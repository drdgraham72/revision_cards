GCSE Maths — Exam Technique & Key Formulae

OCR J560 · Higher Tier · What to memorise & how to maximise marks
Section 1: Command Words & What They Mean
Know exactly what each command word demands — marks depend on it
→
"Show that" — the answer is given; you must prove it step-by-step. Every line of working must be visible. If you skip a step, you lose marks even though the answer is right.
→
"Prove" — formal logical argument. Start from known facts, work to the required result. No gaps in reasoning. State any theorems or rules you use.
→
"Hence" — you MUST use your previous answer. Using a different method = 0 marks, even if your answer is correct.
→
"Hence or otherwise" — they'd prefer you use the previous answer (quicker), but any valid method is accepted.
→
"State" — just write the answer. No working needed. One line. Don't waste time explaining.
→
"Explain" — give mathematical reasoning in words and/or symbols. "Because" must appear. Link cause to effect.
→
"Work out" — show your method and calculation. The working IS the marks — the final answer alone may only score 1 mark.
→
"Estimate" — round every number to 1 significant figure FIRST, then calculate. Using exact numbers = 0 marks.
→
"Give your answer to..." — work with full precision throughout, then round only at the very end. State the rounded answer clearly.
The most common lost marks in GCSE maths come from not reading the command word. "Show that" with a missing step = 0. "Estimate" without rounding = 0.
"Hence" is a gift — it tells you exactly what to use. If you're stuck on a "hence" question, look at your previous answer and think "how does this help?"
Section 2: How to Structure Multi-Mark Questions
The number of marks tells you how many distinct steps the examiner expects
2-mark questions
1
Write the equation or formula you're using
2
Substitute values and give the answer — OR give two distinct observations/reasons
3-mark questions
1
Write the formula or set up the equation
2
Substitute and show intermediate working
3
Final answer with correct units
4–5 mark questions
1
Set up: identify what you need, write the formula or draw a diagram
2
Method: show substitution and algebraic manipulation
3
Intermediate answer: any mid-point result (this often carries its own mark)
4
Final answer: clearly stated, with units, to the precision requested
"Quality of Written Communication" (QWC / asterisk *) questions
→
Write in full sentences, not bullet points
→
Use correct mathematical vocabulary (not "the number on top" — say "numerator")
→
Your logic must flow: statement → reasoning → conclusion
Proof / "Show that" questions
→
Start from the LEFT side of what you're proving (or from given information)
→
Show every algebraic step — no jumps. The examiner needs to see the chain of reasoning.
→
End by writing the statement you were asked to prove, with "as required" or "QED"
In proof questions, you must NOT start from the answer and work backwards. Start from what you know, arrive at what you need to show.
Count the marks, count your steps. If you have a 4-mark question and only 2 lines of working, you're missing something.
Section 3: Formulae You MUST Memorise (Not on OCR Formula Sheet)
OCR does NOT give these — you must know them cold
Geometry — Lengths & Areas
Pythagoras: a² + b² = c²
Trigonometry: SOH CAH TOA sin θ = opp/hyp · cos θ = adj/hyp · tan θ = opp/adj
Area of rectangle = l × w
Area of triangle = ½ × b × h
Area of parallelogram = b × h
Area of trapezium = ½(a + b) × h
Area of circle = πr²
Circumference = 2πr = πd
Geometry — Volumes
Volume of cuboid = l × w × h
Volume of prism = area of cross-section × length
Volume of cylinder = πr²h
Compound Measures
Speed = distance ÷ time
Density = mass ÷ volume
Pressure = force ÷ area
Remember these with triangles: cover what you want, the other two show the operation. D/S×T, M/D×V, F/P×A.
Percentages & Growth
Percentage change = (change ÷ original) × 100
Compound interest: N = N₀ × (1 + r/100)ⁿ
For compound interest: r is the percentage rate, n is the number of time periods. For decay, use (1 − r/100)ⁿ instead.
Probability
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) — for mutually exclusive events
P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B) — for independent events
Algebra & Graphs
nth term of arithmetic sequence: a + (n − 1)d
Gradient = (y₂ − y₁) ÷ (x₂ − x₁)
Equation of a straight line: y = mx + c
For the gradient formula, always label your points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₁) clearly. Subtract in the same order top and bottom — consistency prevents sign errors.
Section 5: Common Mistakes That Lose Marks
Every one of these costs thousands of students marks every year
1
Not showing working — even on calculator papers. If your final answer is wrong but your method is right, working earns you method marks. No working = no method marks = 0.
2
Wrong units or no units — units are often worth a dedicated mark. cm² for area, cm³ for volume, m/s for speed. Check what the question asks for.
3
Premature rounding — use the full calculator display for ALL intermediate steps. Only round at the very end to the precision requested.
4
Ignoring "give your answer to 2 d.p." — if they say 2 decimal places, give exactly 2 decimal places. 3.5 must be written as 3.50.
5
Forgetting ± in quadratic formula — the quadratic formula gives TWO solutions. Write both. If you only give one, you lose a mark.
6
Writing x = −3, 2 instead of x = −3 or x = 2 — use "or" not a comma. A comma implies the pair (−3, 2) which is a coordinate, not two separate solutions.
7
Confusing area and perimeter — area is the space inside (square units). Perimeter is the distance around (linear units). Read the question twice.
8
Forgetting to square the radius — πr² means π × r × r, not π × r. This error appears in circle area, cylinder volume, and sphere calculations.
The single biggest mark-loser across all GCSE maths papers: not showing working. Even if you can do it in your head, WRITE IT DOWN. Method marks exist precisely for this.
Section 4: Formulae GIVEN on the OCR Formula Sheet
You do NOT need to memorise these — they are printed on the front of your exam paper
Quadratic Formula
x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) ÷ 2a
Given on sheet. But you must practise USING it — knowing the formula exists is useless if you can't substitute correctly under pressure.
Trigonometry (Non-Right-Angled Triangles)
Sine rule: a/sinA = b/sinB = c/sinC
Cosine rule: a² = b² + c² − 2bc cosA
Area of triangle = ½ab sinC
3D Shapes
Volume of cone = ⅓πr²h
Surface area of cone = πrl + πr²
Volume of sphere = ⁴⁄₃πr³
Surface area of sphere = 4πr²
Exact Trigonometric Values
Given as a table on the formula sheet sin 0° = 0 · cos 0° = 1 · tan 0° = 0
sin 30° = ½ · cos 30° = √3/2 · tan 30° = 1/√3
sin 45° = √2/2 · cos 45° = √2/2 · tan 45° = 1
sin 60° = √3/2 · cos 60° = ½ · tan 60° = √3
sin 90° = 1 · cos 90° = 0 · tan 90° = undefined
Even though these are given, you should still be familiar with them — you'll work faster if you recognise them without having to look them up every time.
Section 6: Calculator Tips (Papers 4H & 6H)
Your calculator is a powerful tool — learn to use it properly before the exam
Fractions
→
Use the a b/c or □/□ button to enter fractions directly. The display shows proper fractions — no need to convert to decimals.
→
Press S⇔D to toggle between fraction and decimal form. Use this to check your fraction-to-decimal conversions.
Using ANS for Multi-Step Problems
→
After any calculation, press ANS to recall the full-precision result. This avoids rounding errors between steps.
→
Chain calculations: do step 1, press =, then start step 2 using ANS for the previous result. Full precision preserved throughout.
Estimation & Checking
→
After any long calculation, estimate the answer by rounding to 1 s.f. If your calculator answer is wildly different from your estimate, you've made an input error.
→
For equations: substitute your answer back into the original equation to verify it works.
Table Mode for Graphs
→
Use MODE → TABLE to generate y-values for a function. Enter the equation, set start/end/step values, and the calculator produces a complete table of values.
→
This is faster and more accurate than substituting x-values one at a time. Use it for plotting quadratics, cubics, and reciprocal graphs.
Practise these calculator skills BEFORE the exam. Fumbling with buttons under time pressure wastes minutes you cannot afford.
Section 7: Non-Calculator Strategies (Paper 5H)
Paper 5H has no calculator — these mental and written methods are essential
HCF & LCM via Factor Trees
1
Draw a factor tree for each number, breaking down to prime factors
2
HCF: multiply the primes that appear in BOTH trees (take the lowest power of each)
3
LCM: multiply the highest power of every prime that appears in EITHER tree
Multiplication Methods
→
Grid method: split numbers into tens and units, multiply each pair, add results. E.g. 34 × 27: (30×20) + (30×7) + (4×20) + (4×7) = 600 + 210 + 80 + 28 = 918
→
Column multiplication: traditional long multiplication. Line up carefully — one digit shift per row. Add columns at the end.
Division: Bus Stop Method
→
Set up as short/long division. Work left to right: how many times does the divisor go into each group of digits? Carry remainders to the next column.
→
For decimals: add trailing zeros and keep dividing past the decimal point until you get a terminating answer or the required precision.
Fractions ↔ Decimals ↔ Percentages
Fraction → Decimal: divide numerator by denominator
Decimal → Percentage: multiply by 100
Percentage → Fraction: put over 100, then simplify
Key equivalents to memorise ½ = 0.5 = 50% · ¼ = 0.25 = 25% · ¾ = 0.75 = 75%
⅓ = 0.333... = 33.3̄% · ⅕ = 0.2 = 20% · ⅛ = 0.125 = 12.5%
1/10 = 0.1 = 10% · 1/100 = 0.01 = 1%
Exact Trig Values (Must Know for Paper 5H)
No calculator — you must recall these instantly sin 0° = 0 · sin 30° = ½ · sin 45° = √2/2 · sin 60° = √3/2 · sin 90° = 1
cos 0° = 1 · cos 30° = √3/2 · cos 45° = √2/2 · cos 60° = ½ · cos 90° = 0
tan 0° = 0 · tan 30° = 1/√3 · tan 45° = 1 · tan 60° = √3 · tan 90° = undefined
Notice the pattern: sin values go 0, ½, √2/2, √3/2, 1 as the angle increases. Cos values are the reverse. Learn one row and you know both.
Even though these are on the formula sheet for calculator papers, you MUST know them by heart for Paper 5H. No formula sheet will save you there.
Exam Day: Final Reminders
→
Read the question twice. Underline key information: numbers, units, what's being asked for, number of marks.
→
Show ALL working. Even trivial steps. Method marks can rescue a wrong final answer.
→
Check units. If the question asks for cm² and you calculated in m², convert. Units mismatch = lost mark.
→
Use all the time. If you finish early, go back and check. Re-read each question and verify your answer makes sense in context.
→
Don't leave blanks. Write something for every question. Even a diagram or a formula can earn a mark. A blank answer is always 0.
→
Diagrams aren't decoration. If a geometry question has a diagram, annotate it: mark angles, label sides, add construction lines. This helps you think and earns marks.
Three papers: Paper 4H (calculator, 1h30), Paper 5H (non-calculator, 1h30), Paper 6H (calculator, 1h30). Total: 300 marks. Every mark matters.
