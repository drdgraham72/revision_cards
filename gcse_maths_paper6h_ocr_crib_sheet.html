Algebra & Proof
Algebraic Fractions
Add/subtract fractions by factorising denominators into two brackets, then finding a common denominator.
To add a/(x+2)(x+3) + b/(x+3)(x+1):
Common denom = (x+2)(x+3)(x+1)
Multiply each numerator by the missing bracket
Steps: 1) Factorise each denominator fully. 2) Find LCD. 3) Multiply numerators accordingly. 4) Expand & simplify the numerator. 5) Factorise result if possible.
Always factorise denominators FIRST — never cross-multiply without checking for common factors.
If the question says "simplify", your final answer should be factorised. Check if the numerator cancels with any denominator bracket.
Clip 210
Index Laws
Simplify expressions using the laws of indices, including fractional and negative indices.
a^m × a^n = a^(m+n)
a^m ÷ a^n = a^(m−n)
(a^m)^n = a^(mn)
a^(1/n) = ⁿ√a
a^(−n) = 1/a^n
a^0 = 1
ExampleSimplify 2x³ × 4x⁻² = 8x¹ = 8x
Evaluate 27^(1/3) = ³√27 = 3
a^(1/2) means square root, NOT "divide by 2". The denominator of the fraction is the root.

Algebraic Proofs
Use algebra to prove general statements about integers, odd/even numbers, or divisibility.
Even number: 2n
Odd number: 2n + 1
Consecutive integers: n, n+1, n+2
Consecutive evens: 2n, 2n+2, 2n+4
Structure: 1) Define variables. 2) Write the expression algebraically. 3) Manipulate to show the required property. 4) Conclude with a statement linking to the claim.
ExampleProve the sum of 3 consecutive integers is divisible by 3:
n + (n+1) + (n+2) = 3n + 3 = 3(n+1) — a multiple of 3. ∎
"Hence" or "therefore" must appear in your conclusion — the examiner needs to see you've finished the proof.

Geometry & Trigonometry
Pythagoras' Theorem (2D)
Find the hypotenuse or a shorter side in a right-angled triangle.
a² + b² = c²
Hypotenuse: c = √(a² + b²)
Shorter side: a = √(c² − b²)
Calculator paper — leave intermediate values unrounded. Only round the final answer to the required degree of accuracy.
Check which side is the hypotenuse (longest side, opposite the right angle). If finding a shorter side, you SUBTRACT under the root.
Clip 150
Sine & Cosine Rules
For non-right-angled triangles. Label sides a, b, c opposite angles A, B, C.
Sine rule: a/sinA = b/sinB = c/sinC
(use when you have a matching pair: side + opposite angle)

Cosine rule (find side):
a² = b² + c² − 2bc·cosA

Cosine rule (find angle):
cosA = (b² + c² − a²) / 2bc
When to use which: Sine rule → 2 angles & 1 side, or 2 sides & non-included angle. Cosine rule → 3 sides (find angle), or 2 sides & included angle (find side).
In the cosine rule, "a" is always the side OPPOSITE the angle you're finding. Get the labelling right first.
Check: if the cosine value is negative, the angle is obtuse (between 90° and 180°). This is valid — don't panic.
Clip 202
Angles & Right-Angled Triangles
SOH-CAH-TOA for right-angled triangles. Angles around a point sum to 360°.
sinθ = Opposite / Hypotenuse
cosθ = Adjacent / Hypotenuse
tanθ = Opposite / Adjacent
Label the triangle relative to the angle you're working with. Use inverse functions (sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹) to find angles.
Always check your calculator is in DEGREE mode, not radians.
Clips 45, 150
Area of Sectors
A sector is a "pizza slice" of a circle, defined by an angle θ at the centre.
Area of sector = (θ/360) × πr²
Arc length = (θ/360) × 2πr
ExampleSector with r = 5cm, θ = 72°:
Area = (72/360) × π × 5² = (1/5) × 25π = 5π ≈ 15.7 cm²
If the question gives arc length and asks for area, find the angle first, then use it for area.
Clip 149
Volume of Pyramids
V = ⅓ × base area × perpendicular height
Works for all pyramids and cones (a cone is a circular-based pyramid).
ExampleSquare-based pyramid, base 6cm, height 10cm:
V = ⅓ × 36 × 10 = 120 cm³
If they give the slant height, you'll need Pythagoras to find the perpendicular height first.
Clip 170
Area of Compound Shapes & Parallelograms
Parallelogram: A = base × perpendicular height
Compound shapes: split into rectangles, triangles, semicircles etc.
The perpendicular height of a parallelogram is NOT the slant side — it's the vertical distance between the parallel sides.
Clips 55, 85
Equations of Circles & Tangents
Circle centred at origin: x² + y² = r²
Radius to point (a,b) has gradient: b/a
Tangent at (a,b) is perpendicular: gradient = −a/b
Steps for tangent equation: 1) Find the gradient of the radius to the given point. 2) Take the negative reciprocal for the tangent gradient. 3) Use y − b = m(x − a).
The tangent is always perpendicular to the radius at the point of contact — this is the key fact.

Distance = speed × time

Number & Ratio
HCF & LCM via Prime Factors
Write each number as a product of prime factors using a factor tree or repeated division.
HCF = product of shared primes (lowest powers)
LCM = product of all primes (highest powers)
Example60 = 2² × 3 × 5, 90 = 2 × 3² × 5

LCM = 2² × 3² × 5 = 180

Clips 78–80
Recurring Decimals → Fractions
Let x = 0.̇3̇6̇ (repeating "36")
100x = 36.363636...
100x − x = 36
99x = 36 → x = 36/99 = 4/11
Method: Multiply x by 10^n where n = number of repeating digits. Subtract to eliminate the recurring part. Simplify the fraction.
If digits before the repeat aren't recurring (e.g. 0.1̇6̇), you need TWO equations: multiply by 10 and by 1000, then subtract.

Clip 165
Inverse Proportion
Construct equations where one variable is inversely proportional to another (or its square, cube, root).
y ∝ 1/x → y = k/x
y ∝ 1/x² → y = k/x²
Find k by substituting known values
Exampley ∝ 1/x², when x=2, y=5:
5 = k/4 → k = 20 → y = 20/x²
Inverse proportion means as x increases, y decreases. If both increase together, it's direct proportion — re-read the question.

Graphs & Functions
Cubic Function Graphs
y = ax³ + bx² + cx + d. General shape: S-curve (positive a: bottom-left to top-right; negative a: top-left to bottom-right).
y = x³ → passes through origin, point of inflection at (0,0)
y = x³ + 2 → shifted up 2 units
y = (x−1)³ → shifted right 1 unit
Plot by substituting x values into the equation. Join points with a smooth curve — never use straight line segments.
Don't confuse cubic graphs with quadratic parabolas. Cubics have a twist/inflection point; quadratics are symmetric U or ∩ shapes.
Clip 161
Reciprocal Function Graphs
y = k/x (or y = k/x²)
Two separate curves in opposite quadrants
Never crosses either axis (asymptotes at x=0 and y=0)
For y = 1/x: curves in quadrants 1 and 3 (positive k) or 2 and 4 (negative k).
The graph gets closer to the axes but never touches them — draw the curves approaching the axes smoothly.

Clip 64
Statistics & Probability
Histograms (Unequal Widths)
In a histogram, the y-axis is frequency DENSITY, not frequency.
Frequency density = frequency / class width
Frequency = frequency density × class width
To draw: Calculate frequency density for each class. Bars have no gaps; width = class width, height = frequency density. To interpret: Find frequency from a bar by calculating area (width × height).
The most common error: using frequency on the y-axis. The y-axis MUST be frequency density when class widths are unequal.
To estimate the median from a histogram, find the total frequency, halve it, then count across bars from the left until you reach that cumulative frequency.

Clip 57
Experimental Probability
P(event) = number of times event occurred / total number of trials
More trials → experimental probability gets closer to theoretical probability (the Law of Large Numbers).
ExampleA coin is flipped 200 times, heads appears 112 times.
P(heads) = 112/200 = 0.56 (expected: 0.5)
Clips 57, 125
Expected Results
Expected number = P(event) × number of trials
ExampleP(win) = 0.3, play 150 games:
Expected wins = 0.3 × 150 = 45
Expected results are theoretical averages — you won't always get exactly this number. If asked "is this fair?", compare expected vs actual.
Clip 125
Probabilities as Fractions
Express probabilities as simplified fractions. Remember:
0 ≤ P(event) ≤ 1
P(not A) = 1 − P(A)
All probabilities in a sample space sum to 1
If a question gives probabilities as decimals or percentages and asks for a fraction, convert carefully and simplify fully.
