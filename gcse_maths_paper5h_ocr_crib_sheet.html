Vectors & Transformations
Column Vectors 174, 219
A column vector describes movement: top number = horizontal (right +ve), bottom number = vertical (up +ve).
Vector a = (3, −2) means 3 right, 2 down
Adding: add top numbers together, add bottom numbers together.
Subtracting: subtract each component separately.
Example(2, 5) + (−1, 3) = (1, 8)
(4, 7) − (1, 2) = (3, 5)
Write each component calculation separately — show your arithmetic clearly on non-calculator papers.
Reflection 48
Reflect a shape in a mirror line. Every point must be the same perpendicular distance from the line on the other side.
Always state the mirror line in your answer: "Reflection in the line y = x" or "Reflection in x = −1".
Common error: forgetting to name the mirror line loses marks. Just drawing the shape is not enough.
Rotation 49
Three things needed: centre, angle, direction (clockwise/anticlockwise).
Use tracing paper: pin at centre, rotate to new position.
Missing any one of centre, angle, or direction = lost marks. State all three every time.
Algebra & Equations
Constructing & Solving Equations 135
Form an equation from the problem context, then solve step by step.
1. Define your variable (let x = ...)
2. Write the equation from the words
3. Solve by isolating x
Example"A number doubled plus 5 equals 17"
2x + 5 = 17 → 2x = 12 → x = 6
Non-calc: show every step of rearranging. Write "−5 from both sides" etc. to demonstrate method.
Expanding Triple Brackets 178
Expand two brackets first, then multiply the result by the third.
(a+b)(c+d)(e+f):
Step 1: expand (a+b)(c+d) = ac+ad+bc+bd
Step 2: multiply each term by (e+f)
Example(x+1)(x+2)(x+3)
= (x²+3x+2)(x+3)
= x³+3x²+3x²+9x+2x+6
= x³+6x²+11x+6
Collect like terms carefully — write out every partial product before simplifying. Rushing causes sign errors.
Substituting into Formulae 95
Replace each letter with its given value and evaluate. No calculator — use written methods.
Replace variables → apply BIDMAS → simplify
Write out the substitution in full before calculating. E.g. if v = u + at, write v = 3 + 2×5 = 13.
Simultaneous Equations with Quadratics 211
One linear + one quadratic: substitute the linear into the quadratic.
1. Rearrange linear: y = mx + c
2. Substitute into quadratic
3. Solve resulting quadratic (factorise)
4. Find both x and y values
Exampley = x + 1 and y = x² − 3
x + 1 = x² − 3 → x² − x − 4 = 0
Solve → find both (x, y) pairs
You must find BOTH pairs of solutions. Losing the second pair is a common mistake worth 1–2 marks.
Solving Quadratic Inequalities 212
Solve as equation first, then determine which region satisfies the inequality.
1. Solve x² − 5x + 6 < 0 → (x−2)(x−3) = 0
2. Roots: x = 2 and x = 3
3. Sketch parabola to find where it's below zero
4. Answer: 2 < x < 3
Sketch the parabola — the answer is where the curve is below/above the x-axis depending on the inequality sign.
For x² > k: the answer is TWO separate regions (x < −a OR x > a), not a single range.
Sequences: Position-to-Term 102–104
The nth term of an arithmetic sequence:
nth term = a + (n−1)d
a = first term, d = common difference
Finding the rule: work out the common difference, then adjust for the starting value.
ExampleSequence: 5, 8, 11, 14 ... → d = 3
nth term = 3n + 2 (check: 3×1+2=5 ✓)
Number & Fractions
Recurring Decimals to Fractions 177
Use algebra to eliminate the recurring part.
Let x = 0.363636...
100x = 36.363636...
100x − x = 36
99x = 36 → x = 36/99 = 4/11
Multiply by 10, 100, or 1000 depending on how many digits recur. Subtract to cancel the repeating part.
Always simplify the final fraction. Check by dividing numerator into denominator mentally.
Multiplying Mixed Numbers 73
Convert to improper fractions first, then multiply numerators and denominators.
2⅓ × 1½ = 7/3 × 3/2 = 21/6 = 3½
Cross-cancel before multiplying to keep numbers small. E.g. 7/3 × 3/2: the 3s cancel → 7/1 × 1/2 = 7/2.
Dividing with Fractions 74
KFC: Keep the first fraction, Flip the second, Change ÷ to ×.
3/4 ÷ 2/5 = 3/4 × 5/2 = 15/8 = 1⅞
Always convert mixed numbers to improper fractions before dividing.
Written Division with Decimals 67
Make the divisor a whole number by multiplying both numbers by 10 or 100.
3.64 ÷ 0.4 → 36.4 ÷ 4 = 9.1
(multiply both by 10)
Use bus stop method (short division). Show carries clearly. Line up decimal points in your answer.
Estimating Calculations 91
Round each number to 1 significant figure, then calculate.
Estimate: 389 × 21 ÷ 0.52
≈ 400 × 20 ÷ 0.5 = 8000 ÷ 0.5 = 16000
You must show your rounded values to get the method mark. Don't just write the answer.
Factors & Divisibility 28
Divisibility tests (non-calc essential):
÷2: even last digit. ÷3: digit sum divisible by 3. ÷4: last two digits ÷ 4. ÷5: ends 0 or 5. ÷6: passes ÷2 and ÷3. ÷9: digit sum divisible by 9.
Use factor pairs systematically: check 1, 2, 3, 4... up to √n to find all factors.
Statistics & Probability
Sample Space Diagrams 126
A grid showing all possible outcomes for two events. Count favourable outcomes ÷ total outcomes.
P(event) = favourable outcomes / total outcomes
ExampleTwo dice: 6×6 = 36 outcomes total.
P(total = 7) = 6/36 = 1/6
Draw the full grid — don't try to count in your head. Systematic listing avoids missed outcomes.
Conditional Probability (Venn Diagrams) 204
P(A given B) means: restrict to B, then find A within it.
P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)
= number in both / number in B
"Given that" means you change the denominator to the condition group, not the total.
Tree Diagrams (Dependent Events) 204
When items are not replaced, probabilities change on the second branch.
Multiply along branches (AND)
Add between branches (OR)
ExampleBag: 3 red, 5 blue. Pick 2 without replacement.
P(RR) = 3/8 × 2/7 = 6/56 = 3/28
Denominators decrease by 1 for dependent events. Numerators change too if picking the same colour.
Non-calc: leave as fractions and simplify at the end. Finding common denominators to add fractions is key.
Scatter Graphs & Lines of Best Fit 129
Describe: correlation (positive/negative/none), strength (strong/weak).
Line of best fit: roughly equal points above and below. Use it to interpolate (within data range).
Extrapolation (predicting beyond data range) is unreliable — say so if asked.
Describe relationships in context: "As temperature increases, ice cream sales increase" not just "positive correlation".
Proportion & Percentages
Direct Proportion Equations 199
If y is directly proportional to x: y = kx. Find k using given values.
y ∝ x → y = kx
y ∝ x² → y = kx²
Find k: substitute known pair
Exampley ∝ x², y = 50 when x = 5
50 = k × 25 → k = 2 → y = 2x²
Inverse Proportion Equations 199
If y is inversely proportional to x: y = k/x. As one increases, the other decreases.
y ∝ 1/x → y = k/x
y ∝ 1/x² → y = k/x²
To identify type: direct — both increase together. Inverse — one up, other down.
Percentage Change (Non-Calc) 109, 110
Build up percentages from easy chunks you can calculate mentally.
10% = divide by 10
5% = half of 10%
1% = divide by 100
Build: 17.5% = 10% + 5% + 2.5%
Example35% of £240:
10% = £24, 30% = £72, 5% = £12
35% = £72 + £12 = £84
Show the building blocks — this method gets full marks and avoids decimal multiplication.
Fractions & Percentages of Amounts 86, 87
Fraction of amount: divide by denominator, multiply by numerator
Number as %: (part ÷ whole) × 100
Example¾ of 360 = 360 ÷ 4 × 3 = 90 × 3 = 270
Write 18 as % of 60: 18/60 × 100 = 30%
Non-calc: simplify the fraction first. 18/60 = 3/10, and 3/10 × 100 = 30%.
Finding Original Values (Reverse %) 110
After a percentage change, find the original by dividing by the multiplier.
After 20% increase, price is £180:
120% = £180 → 1% = £1.50 → 100% = £150
Do NOT find 20% of £180 and subtract — that gives the wrong answer because the % is of the original, not the new value.
Compound Interest 164
Amount = P × (1 + r/100)ⁿ
P = principal, r = rate, n = years
Non-calc: multiply year by year.
Example£500 at 10% for 3 years (non-calc):
Yr1: £500 × 1.1 = £550
Yr2: £550 × 1.1 = £605
Yr3: £605 × 1.1 = £665.50
For non-calc compound interest, calculate each year separately. Show each multiplication step.
Equivalent Ratios 73, 74
Find equivalent ratios by multiplying or dividing both sides by the same number.
Unitary method: divide to find 1 part, then scale
3:5 = 6:10 = 12:20
Share in a ratio: add parts, divide total by sum of parts, multiply by each part.
Geometry & Measures
Speed, Distance, Time 142
Speed = Distance ÷ Time
Distance = Speed × Time
Time = Distance ÷ Speed
Use the DST triangle: cover what you want to find. Non-calc: convert minutes to fractions of hours (e.g. 45 min = ¾ hr).
Units must match! If speed is km/h, distance must be km and time must be hours.
Density 142
Density = Mass ÷ Volume
Mass = Density × Volume
Volume = Mass ÷ Density
Same triangle approach as speed. Units: g/cm³ or kg/m³. Convert carefully without a calculator.
Arc Length & Sectors 167
Arc length = (θ/360) × 2πr
Sector area = (θ/360) × πr²
Non-calc: leave answers in terms of π unless told otherwise. Simplify the fraction θ/360 first.
ExampleArc length, r = 6, θ = 120°:
= (120/360) × 2π × 6 = (1/3) × 12π = 4π cm
Sine Rule 167
a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C
(or flipped: sin A/a = sin B/b)
Use when you have a side and its opposite angle, plus one other piece.
Use the version with the unknown on top to avoid rearranging.
Exact Trig Values 167
Must be memorised for non-calculator:
sin 0°=0, sin 30°=½, sin 45°=√2/2, sin 60°=√3/2, sin 90°=1
cos 0°=1, cos 30°=√3/2, cos 45°=√2/2, cos 60°=½, cos 90°=0
tan 0°=0, tan 30°=√3/3, tan 45°=1, tan 60°=√3, tan 90°=undef
These WILL be tested on non-calculator. Learn the pattern: sin and cos are mirrors of each other.
Volume of Cones & Spheres 171, 169
Cone: V = ⅓πr²h
Sphere: V = ⁴⁄₃πr³
Hemisphere: V = ⅔πr³
Formulae are given on the exam paper, but practice using them without a calculator — leave in terms of π.
Similar Shapes: Area & Volume 144
If the linear scale factor is k:
Lengths: × k
Areas: × k²
Volumes: × k³
ExampleScale factor = 3 → Area factor = 9, Volume factor = 27
If small surface area = 20cm², large = 20 × 9 = 180cm²
Find the LINEAR scale factor first from corresponding lengths, then square or cube it.
Constructing Loci & Bisectors 146
Perpendicular bisector: compass arcs from each end of line, join intersections.
Angle bisector: arc from vertex, then arcs from where it hits each arm, join to vertex.
Leave all construction arcs visible — they are worth marks. Use a sharp pencil and don't rub out arcs.
Locus of points equidistant from two points = perpendicular bisector. Equidistant from two lines = angle bisector.
Graphs & Functions
Exponential Functions 194
Graphs of y = aˣ where a > 1: curve rises steeply, always above x-axis, passes through (0, 1).
y = aˣ → y-intercept is always (0, 1)
The x-axis is an asymptote (curve never touches it)
To sketch: plot (0,1), (1,a), (2,a²) and note the curve approaches but never reaches y = 0.
Growth & Decay 194
Growth: y = a × (multiplier)ᵗ where multiplier > 1
Decay: y = a × (multiplier)ᵗ where 0 < multiplier < 1
ExampleBacteria doubles every hour, starts at 500:
y = 500 × 2ᵗ
After 3 hours: 500 × 8 = 4000
For decay, the multiplier = 1 − (rate as decimal). E.g. 15% decay → multiplier = 0.85.
Trigonometric Graphs 195
Know the shapes of sin x, cos x, and tan x.
sin x: starts at 0, period 360°, range −1 to 1
cos x: starts at 1, period 360°, range −1 to 1
tan x: period 180°, asymptotes at 90°, 270°...
cos x is just sin x shifted 90° left. tan x repeats every 180° and has vertical asymptotes.
Read the scale on the x-axis carefully — it might be in radians (π, 2π) not degrees.
Non-Calculator Exam Technique
Show Working
Every line of working can earn a method mark. Even if your final answer is wrong, clear working can score 3 out of 4 marks on a question.
Write one step per line. Don't try to do multiple operations in your head at once.
Mental Arithmetic Strategies
Multiplication: break into factors. 24 × 15 = 24 × 10 + 24 × 5 = 240 + 120 = 360.
Division: use chunking or short division. Show the bus stop method clearly.
Fractions: find common denominators by listing multiples or using LCM.
Don't leave answers as decimals if the question asks for exact values — use fractions or surds.
Check Your Answers
Substitute your answer back into the original equation or context to verify.
Use estimation to check: if you calculated 47 × 83 = 3901, estimate 50 × 80 = 4000. Close enough — probably right.
